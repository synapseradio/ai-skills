Your draft breaks three of the four rules a good question needs to follow:

- **Smuggled premise.** "Wasn't it really hard" and "that difficult teammate" tell the candidate what to feel and how to characterize the other person before they've said a word. A candidate who didn't find it hard, or doesn't think the teammate was the problem, now has to either agree with your framing or awkwardly correct you.
- **Two ideas forced into one.** "Wasn't it hard" and "how did you keep it from blowing up" are actually two separate questions — an assessment and an action — joined by "and." The candidate has to pick one to answer, and you lose the other.
- **Leading/self-answering.** "Wasn't it..." already supplies the answer ("yes, it was hard"). You're asking them to confirm your read rather than give you theirs.

Cleaner version:

> "Tell me about a time you had a conflict with a teammate. What happened, and how did you handle it?"

This drops the judgment ("difficult," "blowing up"), lets them define what counted as conflict and how serious it was, and gives you one open lane to walk down rather than two competing ones.
