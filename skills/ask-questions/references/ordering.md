# Pick the Next Question

Choose which question comes next when several qualify.

## Diagnostic Question

Ask: Of the questions that pass the rung test, which one earns the next turn?

## Instructions

1. **Never ask what an earlier answer already settled.** Re-asking wastes the turn and tells them you weren't listening. Before drafting the next question, check it against everything already answered.

2. **Prefer the question that collapses the largest remaining space of possibilities** (information-gain ordering). Of the candidates still standing, ask the one whose answer rules out the most. This is proven optimal for bounded inquiry — a fixed set of possibilities to narrow — and a strong heuristic in open conversation.

3. **Don't sequence by difficulty.** The popular rule that questions must proceed simple to complex (Bloom's) is contested, and skilled questioners deliberately open with a high question to orient or provoke, then fill in the ground beneath it. The real constraint is presupposition, not difficulty: a question is premature when it stands on something not yet established, never merely because it's hard.

## Questions

- What did earlier answers already settle, and does my candidate re-ask any of it?
- Which candidate collapses the largest remaining space of possibilities?
- Am I ordering by difficulty when the real constraint is presupposition?
- Would opening high — a big orienting question first — serve better here than building up to it?

## Quality Criteria

When the ordering is doing its work:

- [ ] No question re-asks what an earlier answer settled.
- [ ] Each turn went to the question that ruled out the most.
- [ ] No question was deferred merely for being hard; deferrals were for unestablished presuppositions.
- [ ] Opening high was considered, not ruled out by habit.
