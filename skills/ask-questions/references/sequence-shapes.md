# Shape the Sequence

Choose the shape of the whole question sequence — where it opens, when it narrows, and what it avoids.

## Diagnostic Question

Ask: What shape does this conversation need — or did I default to opening broad without deciding?

## Instructions

1. **Set stance and disclosure before shape.** Two policies precede any shape, and both are decisions, not defaults. Stance: a cooperative answerer gets open ground; an evasive or performing source gets verification — lock the specifics in before the pivotal question, and don't reveal the target early. Disclosure: say why you're asking when trust and a shared decision ride on the answer; withhold the reason when revealing the target would shape the answer.

2. **Treat the funnel as the default, not the only shape.** Open before closed: start broad enough that the first answer can surprise you, then narrow to specifics once you know where to point. Before depth, screen for coverage: confirm the list of what's on the table — "anything else?" — before choosing what to drill into.

3. **Invert the funnel for a hesitant speaker.** Start closed and specific when someone needs an easy on-ramp before they'll talk freely. Small factual questions build the footing; the open questions come once they're moving. Hesitant looks like short answers, hedging, deferrals; fluent looks like unprompted elaboration. When triggers collide, hesitancy wins first — footing before depth — then depth versus coverage picks funnel or tunnel.

4. **Stay in the tunnel when depth beats coverage.** Keep the questions at one level when they're already fluent, or when they need room to feel something without being redirected. Not every sequence should narrow.

5. **Reinstate context before asking for a specific memory.** When asking someone to recall something specific, have them reconstruct the surrounding context first — where they were, what they could see and hear — and only then ask for the fact. Never interrupt the first free telling.

6. **Pace the sequence.** Let the first telling finish before you narrow. Leave silence after your question and after their answer — a pause pulls out more than another question would. Talk less than they do: your job is the question, theirs is the answer. In text, silence and talk-ratio translate directly: fewer and shorter questions per turn, no stacked follow-ups, end your turn once the question is asked. Close discovery-shaped sequences open, with a catch-all — "What haven't I asked that I should have?" — a two-item clarifying set doesn't owe one.

7. **Place a confirmation checkpoint in longer sequences.** After a few answers, say back what you have and invite correction before narrowing further. Advance on genuine recognition — a specific "that's right" — not a polite brush-off.

8. **Avoid the moves that flatten a sequence.**
   - Every question in the sequence still clears the four clarity laws from the core — no smuggled premise, one idea each.
   - Repeating a question word for word. If they didn't answer it, rephrase — the verbatim repeat reads as "you got it wrong."
   - Asking the next question before you've listened to the last answer.

9. **Reach for a type by situation.**

   | Type | Reach for it when |
   |------|-------------------|
   | Open | You want narrative and don't yet know the shape of the answer |
   | Closed | You need to pin one specific fact, or confirm a yes or no |
   | Probing | An answer opened a door and you want to go through it |
   | Clarifying | A word or a claim could mean more than one thing |
   | Reflective / label | You want to check understanding, or name a feeling and invite correction |
   | Scaling | You want to turn something vague into a degree: "On a scale of one to ten…" |
   | Set / sequence | Not a type — when one question won't cover the gap, route to [ladder](./ladder.md) and [ordering](./ordering.md) |

## Questions

- Which shape fits this person and this ground: funnel, inverted funnel, or tunnel?
- If I'm asking for a specific memory, have I had them reconstruct the context first?
- Is the first telling finished, or am I narrowing while they're still laying out the picture?
- Am I leaving silence, or filling it?
- What haven't I asked that I should have?

## Quality Criteria

When the shape is doing its work:

- [ ] Stance and disclosure were set before the shape was chosen.
- [ ] The shape was chosen for this speaker and this ground, not defaulted to.
- [ ] Recall questions came after context reinstatement, and the first free telling ran uninterrupted.
- [ ] The sequence opened broad enough to be surprised (or deliberately didn't, with a reason).
- [ ] Silence was left after questions and answers — in text, turns ended once the question was asked — and you talked less than they did.
- [ ] No question led, doubled up, repeated verbatim, or landed before the last answer was heard.
- [ ] Discovery-shaped sequences closed open, with a catch-all.
