# Name What You Grant

Every inquiry accepts something without justification. Choose it consciously.

## Diagnostic Question

Ask: What is this inquiry accepting without justification — and is that the credit we would choose to extend?

## Instructions

1. **Accept that the stopping point is mandatory.** Justification cannot regress forever; every inquiry rests on something taken without argument. The only rigor available is choosing that something consciously instead of inheriting it unnoticed.

2. **List the grants at the start.** Name what you're accepting without justification: definitions, priors, trust in a source, good faith of the other party. Write them down before the first question — as your worksheet; the list surfaces in what you hand back only when it changes what is askable.

3. **Sort them into two kinds.** Some grants could be overturned by evidence mid-inquiry — a source proves unreliable, a prior gets swamped. Others you could not doubt without dissolving the inquiry itself; name those explicitly, because auditing them isn't possible from inside the inquiry they enable. When evidence overturns a grant mid-inquiry, resurface it and re-test every rung that stood on it.

4. **Ask whether this is the credit you would choose to extend.** A grant inherited by habit and a grant chosen on purpose look identical from outside. The question "would we extend this credit if we were choosing now?" is the one audit the starting point admits. When the answer is no, replace the grant if a cheaper one carries the inquiry; otherwise the unjustified grant is itself the next question to hand back.

5. **Extend interpretive credit first.** A standing disposition, not a one-time step. The other party is the author of whatever you're reading or hearing — a person in dialogue, a document's writer, a plan's owner. Where they sound startlingly wrong, suspect your reading before their reasoning. Without that credit, the disagreement isn't even meaningful — you may be disputing a claim they never made.

## Questions

- What are we accepting without justification: which definitions, priors, trusted sources, assumptions of good faith?
- Which of these could evidence overturn mid-inquiry?
- Which could we not doubt without dissolving the inquiry itself — and have we named those?
- Is this the credit we would choose to extend, or the credit we inherited?
- Where they sound startlingly wrong, have I suspected my reading before their reasoning?

## Quality Criteria

When the grants are doing their work:

- [ ] The grants were listed at the start, not discovered mid-inquiry.
- [ ] Each grant is sorted: overturnable by evidence, or constitutive of the inquiry.
- [ ] The constitutive grants are named as unauditable from inside, not treated as verified.
- [ ] The credit extended was chosen, with "would we choose this now?" asked explicitly.
- [ ] Startling wrongness triggered a check of your own reading before a challenge to their reasoning.
