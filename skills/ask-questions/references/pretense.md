# Audit the Pretending Question

Find the question that treats the thing as simpler than it is, and make it pay its debts — idealization and its audits.

## Diagnostic Question

Ask: Which question in this inquiry is pretending — treating the object as simpler than it is — and where is its audit?

## Instructions

1. **Expect a pretending question.** Most inquiries contain one: a question that deliberately treats the thing as simpler than it is — "what does this look like if it behaves simply?" That question is often the most productive rung on the ladder, and it is licensed only with its audits.

2. **Classify the simplification.** Is it a scaffold you'll remove once the real structure stands, or a claim that the removed parts don't matter? The two owe different debts: a scaffold owes a removal plan, a claim owes a defense of what it discarded.

3. **Hunt tigers, not mice.** Ask where the simple picture is importantly wrong — the error that would change the conclusion, not the small deviation that wouldn't. Then name which later question is responsible for catching it. A pretense with no audit rung above it is an unpaid debt.

4. **Find the simplest case that breaks it.** Look for the smallest, plainest situation where the simple picture fails. When you find one, ask which hidden assumption it exposed — the repaired claim must now state that assumption out loud.

5. **Never define the breaking case away.** Redrawing the boundaries so the counterexample no longer counts protects the fiction instead of auditing it. The breaking case is information; absorb it into the claim.

## Questions

- Which question here is pretending?
- Is the simplification a scaffold we'll remove, or a claim that the removed parts don't matter?
- Where is the simple picture importantly wrong, and which later question is responsible for catching it?
- What's the simplest case that breaks it, and which hidden assumption did it expose?
- Has the repaired claim stated that assumption out loud, or has the breaking case been defined away?

## Quality Criteria

When the audit is doing its work:

- [ ] The pretending question was located, not assumed absent.
- [ ] The simplification was classified — scaffold or claim — and its debt named accordingly.
- [ ] The hunt targeted errors that would change the conclusion, not trivial deviations.
- [ ] A specific later question owns the job of catching the pretense's failure.
- [ ] Breaking cases were absorbed into the claim, never defined away.
