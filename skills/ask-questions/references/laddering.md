# Climb to What Matters

Reach the value under a stated preference by asking what matters about each answer — the laddering interview, from means-end chain research.

## Diagnostic Question

Ask: Do I have what they prefer, but not why it matters to them?
The stated preference is the bottom rung. The value it serves sits several answers up.

## Instructions

1. **Ask what matters about the answer you just got.** Then ask it again of the new answer, and again. Each pass climbs one rung from attribute toward consequence toward value.

2. **Let the chain decide where to stop.** The climb terminates at something that resists a further "why" — not at your patience. If the last answer still names a means to something else, there's another rung.

3. **Vary the phrasing each rung.** Never repeat the question verbatim. "What does that give you?", "What would having that mean?", "What's important about that?" all climb the same rung in different words.

4. **Watch for fatigue.** The repetition wears people down. When their answers shorten or their tone flattens, respect their limits — pause the climb or come back another way.

5. **Watch for performed rationality.** Under repeated probing, people construct justifications to look reasonable rather than report what actually drives them. When answers turn abstract or suspiciously tidy, anchor to a concrete past instance: "When did that last happen? Walk me through it."

6. **Unblock a stalled climb with the documented moves** (Reynolds & Gutman's own list): evoke the situation it happens in; ask what it would be like to lack the thing, or not to feel that way; ladder the negative — why do you NOT do or want certain things?; contrast with an earlier time in their life; shift to third person when the ground is sensitive; and run the communication check — repeat back what they said and ask for clarification.

7. **Soften "why" on sensitive ground.** A bare "why" reads as a demand to justify, and people defend instead of explain. Reach for "what led to that?" or "how did that come about?" — same information, none of the accusation.

## Questions

- What matters to them about the last answer?
- Has the chain stopped moving on its own, or have I just stopped pushing?
- Am I about to repeat a phrasing verbatim?
- Are the answers turning abstract — do I need a concrete past instance?
- If the climb has stalled, which unblocking move fits this stall?

## Quality Criteria

When the climb is doing its work:

- [ ] Each rung asked what mattered about the previous answer, in fresh words.
- [ ] The climb ended where the chain resisted a further "why," not where patience ran out.
- [ ] Abstract or tidy answers were anchored to concrete past instances.
- [ ] Signs of fatigue were respected rather than pushed through.
- [ ] Sensitive ground got "what led to that?" or third-person phrasing, never a bare "why."
