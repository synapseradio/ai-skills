The framing that's tripping you up is "one-way door." It isn't, and treating it as one is what's making this feel harder than it needs to be with three weeks on the clock.

Here's the actual irreversible resource: the next few months of a six-person team's attention, and however much of your 14 months of runway you burn while they're pointed one direction. The app itself isn't the door. If you stop building consumer features and go quiet on growth, the categorization engine doesn't disappear — you can leave the app running as-is for its existing 2k MAU while you find out whether the API opportunity is real. The door that's actually one-way is committing headcount, a sales motion, support infrastructure, and your board narrative to enterprise before you know the three inbound emails are worth anything.

And right now, you don't know that. Three unprompted companies asking to pay for API access is a genuinely strong signal — most startups never get inbound like that — but it's a signal, not a business. You have three weeks before the board meeting, which is enough time to convert it into evidence instead of walking in with a vibe. Get on calls with all three this week. Find out specifically what they want (raw categorization output? embedded in their product? white-labeled?), what they'd pay, how urgently, and what serving them would actually require — rate limits, docs, uptime guarantees, security review. Push each one toward a concrete commitment: a signed LOI, a paid pilot, even a small one. One real dollar figure from an actual conversation is worth more to your board than the word "pivot."

Two decisions are tangled together here and splitting them will make the choice easier. The first is whether to keep investing in consumer growth — at 2k MAU flat, you likely already have your answer, and you can act on that now regardless of how the API bet plays out. The second is whether to commit fully to enterprise API as the company — hiring for it, building support and compliance around it, letting it define the roadmap. That second one is the real one-way door, and it's the one you should hold open until the pilot conversations give you something firmer than three emails.

One thing worth checking before you say anything to the board: does the categorization engine depend on data or feedback coming from the consumer app — training signal, labeled corrections, usage patterns? If sunsetting the app quietly starves the model you're about to sell, that's a dependency you need to solve first, not discover after the board meeting.

For the meeting itself, I'd bring a staged plan instead of a binary vote. Something like: put the app into maintenance mode — no new feature investment, minimal on-call, existing users unaffected — and put two or three people on a 90-day sprint to productize the API and close one or two of the pilots you're working this week. Reassess with real numbers — signed contracts, actual usage, pipeline beyond the initial three — at the 90-day mark. That preserves most of your runway, gives the board a decision they can actually evaluate instead of a leap of faith, and doesn't force you to answer "should we pivot" before you've done the work that would tell you.

The question to sit with isn't "pivot or don't." It's: after this week's calls, do you have evidence that three logos are the start of a pipeline, or the ceiling of one? That answer, not the app's fate, is what should drive the board conversation.
