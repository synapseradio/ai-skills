Before you sign anything, separate this into the two decisions it actually contains, because a lease and a partnership are different bets with different time horizons.

**Decision one: is the demand real?** Classes selling out is great, but it started in March, right after a news feature. Four months of sold-out classes following a publicity spike doesn't yet tell you whether you have durable demand or a temporary bump that a news mention produces in any small local business. Before Friday, pull your booking data and look at the trend line separately from the spike: were you already close to capacity before the feature ran, or did enrollment jump sharply right after it and has it held steady since, or is it already tapering? If it's tapering, three years of doubled rent on a guess is a different risk than three years of rent on a studio that's been consistently full for months on its own merits.

**Decision two: does the business still work if your co-teacher leaves?** This is the bigger one, because it changes what the second unit is even for. If she moves next year, are you planning to run the expanded space solo, hire a replacement, or contract it back down? Have that conversation with her directly and soon, not as a hypothetical but as a real planning input: does she know a timeline, does she want to sell her share or stay on as a remote partner, would she want to train a replacement before she goes. You don't need certainty from her, you need enough to model two scenarios: the studio with two teachers and the studio with one, and whether the expanded footprint pencils out under both.

A few concrete things to do before Friday:

- Ask the landlord directly whether the three-year term is negotiable, or whether they'd do a shorter initial term with an option to extend, a break clause after year one, or the right to sublet the second unit if things change. Landlords often have more flexibility than the first offer suggests, especially when you're already a known, paying tenant. Worst case they say no and you're where you started.
- Run the numbers for the second unit under three scenarios: sustained demand with both of you teaching, sustained demand with just you, and demand reverting to pre-feature levels. If the lease only works in the best case, that's your answer.
- If the landlord won't budge on term length and the co-teacher's plans are still genuinely unknown, a real option is to say no to this specific lease now and ask if they'll offer similar space again in six to twelve months, once you have more data on both the demand trend and her decision. Losing this particular unit is a real cost, but taking on three years of rent you can't unwind is a bigger one if either uncertainty breaks the wrong way.
