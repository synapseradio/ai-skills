**What am I looking at, and what bounds it?** The choice is how `explore_possibility` — and every module shaped like it — crosses from the prompt-era protocol into typed inference calls. The source (`legacy/lib/prism/thinking_primitives.in`) is already a three-step `sequence`, each step under its own persona (`@curious_explorer`, `self`, `@careful_evaluator`), with the second step reading a value the first step set and the third reading the second's. Three constraints already bound the answer before this question was asked. Principle 3 in `docs/principles.md` says an inference point is a leaf that "does not itself combine, splice, or chain other inferences." Principle 4 says composition — splicing, back-reference — "belongs to the INDRA language, not to host code." Principle 7 says explicitly: "when a port can choose between one large inference and several small chained ones, the chained shape is the one this principle favors." So this isn't three options weighed from neutral ground — the runtime's own written principles already lean hard on one side. The genuinely open part is the "how it ages" question, which is about the future, not the present tradeoff.

**What do I know, assume, must verify, must ask?** I know the pilot ported this exact module both ways (`runtime/experiments/inference-fidelity/baml_src/thinking_primitives.baml`) and that the fused single-call port parsed cleanly on every weak-model generation — the schema held, so this was never a robustness problem. I know from `findings.md`'s D9 section what the fused port actually cost: step sequencing became field order, the two same-stream back-references became field dependencies, and — named as the costliest — the three persona voices blended into one system prompt, destroying real persona switching. I know the chained alternative (three typed calls, runtime passing `wondering` → `developed_thought` → `assessment` as data) restores that switching and fits the `sequence`/`step`/`as:` grammar the interpreter-runtime spec already defines — no new IR needed. I can't ask the asker anything here, so where I'd normally ask I'm stating the assumption instead: that `thinking_primitives` is not a one-off but representative of a real corpus still waiting to port (`legacy/lib/prism/personas/` alone holds seventeen persona files, plus `modules/`, `strategies/`, `techniques/`) — otherwise "how this ages" wouldn't be a meaningful question at all.

**Which unresolvables would change what I recommend?** Two. First, how much of that corpus actually gets ported at real scale versus staying a handful of one-off modules — call it **scale**. Second, if a fused form is ever permitted at all, whether its use stays a reviewed, rare exception or becomes the path of least resistance for whoever's porting the next module under time pressure — call it **governance**. A third candidate, raw latency/cost pressure from chaining, turns out not to be an independent axis: it's a consequence that shows up inside the scale axis rather than a separate fork, so it drops out here and reappears below as a consequence, not a tile-maker.

Both axes vary independently — holding scale at "many modules port" still allows governance to hold or drift, and holding governance at "disciplined" still allows scale to stay small or grow — so both survive to become the two axes, at the sweet spot the method aims for.

**Four tiles, one collapse.** Few-ports-held and few-ports-drifts barely differ in stakes, since neither ever gets tested by volume — call the pair **low stakes**, whichever way governance lands. The two live tiles are **many-ports-held**, where the corpus grows with chaining as the default and fusing reserved for measured exceptions, and **many-ports-drifts**, where fusing becomes how ports "get done" and composition logic quietly recolonizes the model prompts the runtime was built to keep clean of it. That second tile is worth naming exactly: it reproduces, field-by-field, the complaint principle 1 makes against the legacy `next_state_assertion` — a model once again carrying responsibility for coherently sequencing something the runtime should own, just relocated from an explicit assertion into implicit field ordering.

Tracing consequences inside the two live tiles turns up one that both reach regardless of which way governance lands: more chained calls per sequence means more round trips, and enough scale turns that into real latency and generated-function maintenance load. That pressure is close to inevitable once porting reaches real volume, and it's exactly the pressure principle 7 is already anticipating when it allows a "single-call form as an optimization" — so the honest reading is that the fused form has a legitimate future use, just not as a coequal default available from day one. One more link is worth flagging and leaving alone rather than chasing: in the drifting tile, opaque fused prompts get harder to debug, which can slow how fast anyone wants to port the next module, which feeds back into suppressing the very scale axis that got us here. That's a loop, not a chain, so it stops here — it deserves its own systems pass, not more tiles.

**Watchpoints.** For scale: watch whether the next few openspec changes actually start converting `graph_of_thought.in`, `multi_perspective.in`, or any further persona file into typed calls, versus the corpus sitting untouched — that's observable at each change cycle, arrives well before any drift would matter, and cleanly discriminates few from many. For governance: watch whether "support both," if it ships, ships with a gate — a review rule or lint requiring the fused form to carry a measured justification — versus shipping as a bare, ungated option. The presence or absence of that gate is checkable the day the option lands, long before usage patterns would otherwise reveal drift, and it's the single most discriminating signal for which of the two live tiles is arriving.

**Which move holds up.** Scoring the three options: fusing always is exposed the moment scale arrives, because it can't coexist with a "held" governance state at all — reviewers citing principles 3, 4, and 7 would have grounds to reject it, so it structurally cannot reach the good tile, only the bad one. Supporting both, offered as a coequal choice today, is live in *both* many-ports tiles — its outcome depends entirely on a governance mechanism the runtime doesn't yet have any tooling to enforce, which makes it a bet on discipline holding under pressure with nothing currently backing that bet. Chaining by default is the one move that changes the tile map itself: drift requires a fused escape hatch to drift toward, so if chaining is the standing default and fusing isn't offered as a parallel option, the drifting tile has nothing to land in — it collapses into the held tile by construction, not by hoping reviewers stay vigilant.

So chain by default, and treat a fused path not as a second first-class port style to build now, but as a later, gated exception — introduced only once a specific chained sequence is profiled as a real latency or cost hot path, and only with an explicit note on which guarantees it's trading away (persona-switch fidelity, per-step observability). That's functionally close to "support both," but sequenced: it removes the temptation to reach for fusing out of convenience on day one, while leaving room to pay the latency cost down later with evidence instead of foreclosing it. The weakest tile for this recommendation is the one where scale is real and chaining's round-trip cost genuinely bites — that cost is real and worth tracking against the scale watchpoint above, but it's a maintenance tax to manage, not a failure mode that kills the recommendation the way fusing-as-default dies against principles 3, 4, and 7, or the way an ungated "both" dies against the governance axis having no enforcement today.
