**Q1 — what am I looking at, and what bounds it?** Six people, one product with 2k MAU that hasn't moved, and a categorization engine underneath it that three separate companies have asked to pay for. Two clocks: 14 months of runway, which is close to a hard constraint — no amount of good planning invents more of it — and a board meeting in 3 weeks, which is softer than it looks. The board needs a credible plan in 3 weeks, not necessarily an irreversible commitment in 3 weeks, and that gap matters. The actual one-way door here is sunsetting the app: losing the 2k users, whatever brand and support history comes with them, and any chance to revisit the product. Talking to three companies about paid API access, or running one cheap experiment on the app, isn't a one-way door at all — both are close to free to attempt and easy to walk back.

**Q2 — what do I know, and what do I still need to ask or verify?** Known: team size, runway, the flat MAU line, and three inbound requests to pay for API access. A few things matter enough to name even without anyone in the room to answer them, so here's what I'd ask before the board meeting, with the assumption I'm running with in its place: Have the three companies mentioned budget or timeline, or just interest? Assumed unconfirmed — treat it as expressed interest, not committed spend, until someone actually quotes a price. Does the categorization engine need live traffic from the consumer app to keep improving, or can it run and keep getting better on data you already have? This is a fact your own engineers can check this week, not a genuine uncertainty — it doesn't belong on an axis. Assumed for this analysis: the engine can be served and retrained independently of the app. If that's wrong, it overrides everything below, so it's the first thing to confirm before you present anything to the board. What's the actual cost split between running the app and running the engine? Assumed: the engine's infrastructure is a sunk cost either way, so serving three API customers is closer to marginal spend than a second product's worth of new burn. Is the app's flatness a distribution problem — people aren't finding it — or a retention problem — people try it and leave? Genuinely unconfirmed, and it changes the answer, so it becomes an axis rather than an assumption.

Two things survive every check available here: how durable the paid API interest really is, and whether the app's flat line is a fixable distribution problem or an actual ceiling.

**Q3 — would either of those change what you do?** Yes to both. If the API interest turns out to be three companies wanting a one-off integration rather than a repeatable business, staking the company on it while killing the one asset with real users would be reckless. If the app's flatness is a distribution problem nobody's actually fixed yet, sunsetting it forecloses a lever you never pulled. Two axes — the point where this method earns its keep.

**Q4 — do they move independently?** Yes. Hold API demand at "durable": the app can still be salvageable or genuinely stuck either way, since that's a separate market with its own dynamics. Hold the app at "a dead end": three companies wanting the API doesn't depend on what the consumer product does next. No shared root cause, so both stand as their own axes.

**Q5 — which tiles survive?** Four, and none of them get killed. None contradicts the runway, the team size, or anything already in motion, and each holds up under the pressure of its other axis. All four carry forward:

- **Clean pivot** — the API demand is durable, and the app really has hit a ceiling.
- **Amputating a working limb** — the API demand is durable, but the app's flatness is a fixable distribution problem, untested.
- **Mirage** — the API interest turns out shallow, and the app has also hit its ceiling.
- **Wrong problem** — the API interest is shallow, but the app has a real, fixable lever sitting unused.

**Q6 — does every plausible near-term future land in exactly one of these?** Yes — no gaps, no overlaps.

**Q7 — what follows inside each tile, and what holds across most of them?** One consequence shows up regardless of which tile turns out to be true: getting one of the three companies to a small, paid pilot in the next few weeks is cheap, fast, and tells you which world you're in — without requiring you to sunset anything first. That's worth doing in every tile, starting now, independent of whatever the board decides.

**Q8 — what would tell you which tile is arriving, early enough to matter?** Two watchpoints, one per axis. For API demand: does at least one of the three companies actually sign and pay for a pilot within a few weeks of getting a real quote, or does the conversation stall, ask for something free, or go quiet? A prompt signature says durable; stalling or "can we get this free" says shallow. For the app: does one sharp, cheap, two-week distribution experiment — a new acquisition channel, a positioning change, the one feature that's been sitting in the backlog — move activation or MAU at all? Movement says the lever is real; nothing moving after a genuinely good attempt says the ceiling is real.

**Q9 — which move survives every tile?** Three candidates: pivot fully now — sunset the app, commit the whole team to the API business before the board meets; hold steady — keep running the app as-is and treat the three emails as a nice-to-have; or run a short, dual-track test — spend the three weeks before the board meeting getting one of the three companies to a signed pilot and running one cheap distribution experiment on the app, then bring the board a decision built on real evidence with a hard follow-up date, instead of a guess dressed as conviction.

Pivoting fully now thrives in Clean pivot but dies in the other three — it forecloses a lever that turns out to matter in two of them, and it burns the one thing you have (users) for demand that hasn't been tested in the third. Holding steady never dies, but never thrives either — it protects the option without ever cashing in the API interest that's sitting in your inbox right now. The dual-track test thrives in three tiles and merely survives in Clean pivot, where it costs a few extra weeks to confirm what would've been the right call anyway. Nothing here routes to "buy more time and decide nothing" — the dual-track test already is the committed move, with an end date attached.

Your board question splits on two things nobody in the room can answer from memory: whether the paid interest in your engine is a real, repeatable business, and whether your app's flat line is a wall or just an unpulled lever.

In **Clean pivot**, the three emails turn into signed pilots fast, and a focused two-week push on the app moves nothing — confirming what the flat line already suggested. Here, selling the engine really is the business, and the app was never coming back. In **Amputating a working limb**, the same paid interest shows up, but that same two-week push on the app produces real movement — meaning the thing you were about to shut down had a lever nobody had pulled yet, and killing it now would be throwing away a second business to fund a first one that didn't need the sacrifice. **Mirage** is the one nobody wants: the three companies stall or ask for something free, and the app experiment goes nowhere either — neither line is a business yet, only two threads worth pulling before anyone commits to anything permanent. **Wrong problem** is the quieter trap: the API interest turns out to be one-off curiosity, but the app genuinely responds to the right push, meaning the actual growth story was sitting in a backlog the team hadn't gotten to.

One thing holds no matter which of these you're living in: turning one of the three inbound emails into an actual paid pilot in the next few weeks costs you almost nothing and tells you more than any amount of internal debate will. Start that now, in parallel with everything else.

Two hinges decide which story you're actually in, both readable before the board meeting closes out its follow-up window. Whether one of the three companies signs and pays for a pilot promptly, versus stalling or asking for free access, tells you if the API demand is durable or shallow. Whether a sharp, two-week distribution experiment on the app moves activation or MAU at all, versus nothing happening, tells you if the app's ceiling is real or just unaddressed.

**Recommendation:** don't sunset anything at the board meeting in three weeks. Walk in with a paid pilot already signed or in motion with at least one of the three companies, and the results of one real distribution experiment on the app — not a plan to run these things, the actual results. Ask the board to bless a short, dated window (six to eight weeks) to finish both reads, with a named decision date at the end of it, rather than a definitive pivot-or-hold vote today. This move survives every version of the story above; it never dies anywhere. Its only real cost shows up in Clean pivot, the world where a full pivot now would've been the right call anyway — there, the dual-track approach just spends a few extra weeks confirming what you'll end up doing regardless. That's a cheap price for not finding out, at month ten of fourteen, that you killed the wrong half of the company.
