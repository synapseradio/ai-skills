# Compose Workflow

Blank-page composition from intent. Where the Clarify and Redraft workflows improve existing prose, the Compose workflow creates prose from purpose — generative, not reactive. The workflow is explicitly recursive: drafting often reveals the real argument, so it loops back when discovery happens.

If a thought resists English expression mid-workflow, read [workflow-bridge.md](./workflow-bridge.md) for the stuck passage, then return.

## Why Composition Needs Its Own Workflow

Improvement tools assume prose already exists. They diagnose, repair, refine. But the hardest writing problem is not "make this better" — it is "make this exist." Composition requires a different workflow: surface the necessity, find the position, discover the argument through drafting, then shape what emerged. No amount of clarity technique fixes a piece that never found its reason to exist.

## Recursive Shape

```text
Frame --> Structure --> Compose
  ^                       |
  +------ Discover <------+
              |
           Polish
```

If the draft changed what you know, return to Frame. This is the skill working — not the writer failing. Every tradition studied describes writing as discovery: Murakami enters novels without a plan, Ernaux calls her method "an ethnology of myself," Saunders describes losing himself entirely in revision. The loop is the composition process.

## Phase 0: Orient

Before any other phase, identify the rhetorical tradition the writer is working in or for. This modifies how structure questions are asked in Phase 2 — Arabic parallel-restatement, French dialectical construction, Japanese indirect development, and Anglo-American linear-deductive organization all produce different structural logic.

Load tradition lens first. All downstream phases operate within the identified tradition, not against an invisible Anglo-American baseline.

Read [across-languages.md](./across-languages.md).

## Phase 1: Frame

Establish the generative foundation. Four questions, in this order:

1. **Necessity** (mandatory first): Why must this be written? What would be lost if it were not? Without necessity, there is only content production. Do not proceed to Structure until the writer can name what compels this piece to exist. Load fit to assess whether the intended form serves this purpose — does the shape match the goal?

2. **Purpose**: What must this accomplish, and for whom? Purpose may be discovered through drafting — it does not need to be final here, but it needs a first articulation.

3. **Authorial stance** (mandatory, not optional): Who speaks, and from what relationship to this subject? Three questions must be answered:
   - "What is my relationship to this subject?"
   - "What can I legitimately claim?"
   - "What am I unable to see from where I stand?"

   Authorial stance is not a disclaimer — it is a structural decision that shapes what the writing can and should do.

4. **Audience and tradition**: What does the audience already know? What rhetorical conventions do they expect? Load calibrate to assess depth and convention fit.

**Output of Frame:** necessity statement, purpose statement, positional acknowledgment, audience/tradition context.

For extended Frame guidance (necessity questions from Luiselli, Ngugi, Ernaux; position framework; tradition identification; audience analysis), read [frame-guide.md](./frame-guide.md).

## Phase 2: Structure

Based on the rhetorical tradition identified in Orient, determine organizational logic.

1. **Organizational fit**: What structural pattern serves this tradition and purpose? Load tradition for structural options by tradition (linear-deductive, parallel-accumulative, dialectical, indirect-implicative, spiral/recursive).

2. **Arc shape**: What creates pull — what tension, question, or promise draws the reader forward? Load arc.

3. **Information flow**: What must come first to make what follows possible? Load clarify for prerequisite-trace — identify concepts that must be introduced before they are referenced.

Structure and Compose are bidirectional. Go to Compose when ready; return to Structure when the draft reshapes the argument.

## Phase 3: Compose

Generative drafting. Follow the surprising sentence — when the prose goes somewhere unexpected, attend to it rather than correcting course. This is where discovery happens.

Before suggesting additions, ask whether gaps are doing rhetorical work — intentional omission, strategic silence, productive ambiguity. Not every gap needs filling; some gaps create meaning.

**Embodied specificity**: Ground claims in situated, witnessed detail. Load illustrate for both concrete examples and grounding moves — before reaching for an abstract noun, ask what particular sensory experience could replace the category.

**Illustration**: When claims need concrete instances, supply them from the specific world of the piece, not from generic examples.

Allow contradiction to surface during Compose. Do not smooth it yet — the Discover phase is where the writer decides whether contradiction is the point.

Read [illustrate.md](./illustrate.md).

## Phase 4: Discover

Self-audit after each draft. Three key questions:

1. **Did this draft teach you something you didn't know when you started?** If the draft surfaced new understanding, that is the composition process working.

2. **Does your Frame still hold?** If drafting revealed the real argument — if necessity, purpose, or position shifted — return to Phase 1 with the new understanding. This loop is not revision failure; it is the skill working correctly.

3. **What premises emerged that were not in the Frame?** Load surface-assumptions to identify what the draft now takes for granted that was never established.

Calibrate what the draft claims against what is known. Load signal-confidence to check whether certainty levels match evidence.

Read:

- [surface-assumptions.md](./surface-assumptions.md)
- [signal-confidence.md](./signal-confidence.md)

**Decision point:** If Frame has shifted, return to Phase 1. If Frame holds, proceed to Polish.

## Phase 5: Polish

Final craft and audience connection. Five dimensions:

1. **Voice**: Consistent authorial presence that speaks from the stated position. Does this sound like one person wrote it? Does that person speak from a stated position — or does the prose slip into generic, unmarked register? Load voice.

2. **Register and slop audit**: Word-level precision, emotional register, and the full slop audit. Run the lexical, structural, voice, casual-context, and intransitive-form checks per [avoid-slop.md](./avoid-slop.md). Compute audit diagnostics: sentence-length CV per paragraph above 0.5; Kobak density at most one per 1,000 words; transitional-phrase ratio at most one per 100 words; em-dash density at most one per paragraph. Each is a hard rule, not a target. Load register and avoid-slop.

3. **Arrange**: Check whether section ordering serves the purpose. Does the sequence build, or merely accumulate? Load arrange for ordering logic.

4. **Audience calibration**: Does depth match what the audience already knows? Do rhetorical conventions match the tradition identified in Orient? Load calibrate.

5. **Analogize**: What unfamiliar concepts appear without anchoring? Load analogize for knowledge bridging through structural analogy. Cross-cultural bridging belongs in the Bridge workflow, not here.

## Techniques Available Throughout

These techniques are not assigned to a specific phase but may be loaded when needed:

- **strengthen** — when language needs more commitment, less cushioning (apply hedging split: distinguish epistemic calibration from habitual cushioning)
- **rhythm** — when sentence-level cadence matters
- **pose-questions** — when the piece should invite exploration rather than close inquiry

## NEVER

1. **Do not compose before Frame completes.** Necessity and purpose must be articulated before Structure begins. If the writer cannot name why this must be written, the workflow's job is to help them find that — not to skip ahead.

2. **Do not invent authorial stance.** If the writer has not specified their relationship to the subject, ask. Do not construct a generic authorial voice — generic voice is not neutral, it is a specific cultural register with its own assumptions.

3. **Do not resolve contradiction in Compose.** Unresolved tension is permitted and encouraged during drafting. The Discover phase is where the writer decides whether contradiction is the point or a problem.

4. **Do not treat the Frame-to-Discover loop as failure.** Normalize discovery. If the draft changed what the writer knows, returning to Frame is the workflow working correctly — not a sign that planning was insufficient.

For cross-cutting rules (hedging split, friction, gaps, L1 patterns, tradition), read [never.md](./never.md).
