## Translanguaging vs. Translation

**Translanguaging** (Garcia & Li Wei, 2014) treats a bilingual person's languages as one integrated cognitive repertoire rather than two discrete systems to switch between. The bilingual thinker draws on their full linguistic resources — grammar, idiom, conceptual frame, rhetorical convention — as a unified system.

**Translation** treats languages as separate containers and optimizes for correctness in the target language. It asks: "What is the English equivalent?" Translation succeeds when the English version sounds natural to a monolingual English reader.

**Translanguaging** asks a different question: "What must survive from the source framing?" It succeeds when the English version preserves the conceptual architecture of the original thought, even at the cost of sounding less "natural" in English.

The difference is not cosmetic. A Spanish speaker who writes "I have hunger" in English gets "corrected" to "I am hungry" by translation logic — losing the externalized relationship to a bodily state that *tener hambre* encodes. The gap is not vocabulary. It is worldview.

This skill operates from the translanguaging perspective: L1 influence is a resource to be understood and preserved where meaningful, not interference to be eliminated (Baruch College Writing Center; Beatty-Martinez et al., 2020).

## The Four Gap Types

### 1. Grammatical Ontology Gap

The native language's grammar encodes a philosophical distinction that English grammar collapses.

**Spanish ser/estar** — Two verbs for "to be" that encode whether a quality is essential (ser) or contingent (estar). "Es triste" means "is sad by nature" — sadness as identity. "Esta triste" means "is sad right now" — sadness as temporary state. English has one "is" for both. When a bilingual thinker writes in English, this distinction vanishes.

**Spanish tener + emotion** — Spanish encodes emotions as things one has (possesses, carries), not things one is. "Tengo miedo" = "I have fear." The self remains separate from the feeling — emotion is something carried, not something that defines. English "I am afraid" collapses self and feeling into identity. This is not a quirk of vocabulary; it reflects a different ontology of selfhood and emotion. (Breeze & Casado-Velarde, 2019; Holmes et al., 2024)

**Japanese particles** — Sentence-final particles encode the speaker's epistemic and relational stance:

- *ne* proposes shared knowledge, invites agreement ("beautiful, isn't it?")
- *yo* asserts information the speaker holds that the listener may not ("it IS beautiful")
- *kana* mitigates, softens, marks doubt — functioning as an epistemic hedge

These particles encode the speaker's relationship to both the claim and the listener simultaneously. English has no grammatical equivalent — it must use separate sentences, adverbs, or tag questions to approximate what a single particle achieves. (Saigo, 2006; Matsugu, 2010)

**Arabic verbal morphology** — Arabic verb forms encode patterns of agency, causation, and reciprocity within the morphological structure itself. Agentless constructions may encode divine or natural causation deliberately — the absence of a named agent is a semantic choice, not a grammatical shortcut.

### 2. Rhetorical Convention Gap

The native tradition organizes argument, evidence, and persuasion using a different logic than English expects.

**Arabic parallel-restatement** — Argument built through repetition, elaboration, and parallel structures rather than thesis-evidence-conclusion. The reader extracts the thesis from the accumulated pattern. This is persuasion through layered restatement, not disorganization. (Kaplan, 1966; Abbadi, 2014)

**French dialectical structure** — The *dissertation* form: state a position, argue against it, then transcend both in synthesis. The writer demonstrates intellectual distance by holding contradictions before resolving them. Personal conviction emerges through dialectical rigor, not direct assertion. Writing "around" a point is method, not evasion. (McKeane, 2023)

**Japanese indirection** — Movement toward implication rather than assertion. The thesis may never be stated directly; it emerges from the arrangement of evidence and the reader's inference. This reflects a high-context epistemology where the reader is trusted to complete the meaning.

### 3. Conceptual Lacuna

The concept exists in L1 but has no direct English lexical equivalent. English approximations carry different connotations.

Examples:

- Portuguese *saudade* — longing for what is absent, with the knowledge that return may be impossible. Not nostalgia (which implies sweetness). Not grief (which implies loss). A present-tense relationship with absence.
- Japanese *wabi-sabi* — beauty located in impermanence, incompleteness, and imperfection. Not "rustic charm." An aesthetic philosophy that locates meaning in transience.
- Arabic *tarab* — musical ecstasy that transcends the act of listening. Not "being moved by music." A state where the boundary between listener and music dissolves.
- Spanish *duende* — a dark, earth-bound creative force. Lorca: "All that has dark sounds has duende." Not inspiration or passion — something wilder and more embodied.
- German *Schadenfreude* — pleasure in another's misfortune. Now borrowed into English, but the borrowing itself demonstrates the gap.

When no clean mapping exists, say so explicitly and offer an explanatory gloss rather than a false equivalent.

### 4. Pragmatic Convention Gap

The native language has different norms for directness, hedging, formality, or relational positioning.

**Japanese relational hedging** — Sentence-final particles and indirect formulations encode social awareness and relational care. "That would be difficult" communicates refusal in Japanese business contexts. Hedging is not weakness — it is sophisticated relational calibration.

**High-context communication** — In high-context traditions (Japanese, Arabic, Chinese, Korean), meaning is distributed between explicit text and shared context. Over-explicitness reads as clumsy or insulting. The skill of leaving space for inference is a feature, not a deficit. (Hall, 1976)

**French epistemic subordination** — Accumulation of subordinate clauses signals intellectual rigor and epistemic care in French academic writing. What reads as "overly complex" in English may be the writer demonstrating that they have considered qualifications and conditions.

## Ontological Framing

The key distinction between this skill and surface-level translation: every annotation names the **ontological** difference, not just the linguistic one.

### The Pattern

| Surface description (avoid) | Ontological description (use) |
|---|---|
| "Spanish uses *tener* here" | "Spanish encodes this emotion as something you carry rather than something you are — the self remains separate from the feeling" |
| "Japanese adds *ne* at the end" | "The particle *ne* proposes that this knowledge is shared between speaker and listener — it positions the claim as communal rather than individual" |
| "Arabic uses passive voice here" | "The agentless construction encodes an action without a human initiator — the cause may be understood as natural, divine, or systemic rather than individual" |
| "French is more indirect" | "The dialectical structure demonstrates intellectual distance — the writer shows they can hold the counter-position before committing, which signals rigor in the French tradition" |

### Why Ontological Framing Matters

Surface descriptions treat L1 patterns as quirks of grammar — things languages "happen to do differently." Ontological descriptions reveal that these patterns encode different relationships to agency, certainty, time, selfhood, and knowledge.

The distinction changes what gets preserved. If ser/estar is "just a grammar thing," it gets collapsed in English without comment. If ser/estar encodes a philosophical distinction about essential vs. contingent qualities, its loss in English becomes something worth naming and annotating.

---

## Why This Matters

Multilingual thinkers are not operating with a deficit. They are navigating between ontologies — different systems for encoding agency, certainty, time, emotion, and social relationship. When the gap between a thought and its English expression is not vocabulary but worldview, the appropriate response is not correction but bridging: preserving what can survive, naming what cannot, and letting the writer choose.

The translanguaging perspective treats L1 influence as a cognitive resource (Beatty-Martinez et al., 2020). Grammatical patterns carried from L1 into English may represent precision that English lacks, not errors that English should correct.
