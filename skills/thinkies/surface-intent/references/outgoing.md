# Outgoing intent — make your own intent legible

The method behind Beat 2. As you produce, put your intent into the artifact so the
next reader does not have to reconstruct it.

## Name things for what they are

A name should carry the thing's purpose, so the reader learns what it does from the
name alone. When you reach for a comment to explain *what* a function or section
does, the name has failed — rename it, and delete the comment. Comments are for
*why*: the reason, the tradeoff, the thing to know before changing it.

## One named root over indirection

When several things share a purpose, the weak move is to wire them together with
pointers — see-also links, cross-references, "as in X." The reader then follows a
trail to assemble an intent no single place states. The strong move is to name the
shared purpose once, and let each thing read as a recognizable instance of it. The
root holds the intent in the open; the instances stay distinct. One named root beats
many links because the reader sees the intent without walking the graph.

## Calibrate for clarity

Clarity is the goal: whoever has to act on this should be able to, on the first
read, without reconstructing what you meant. The test is concrete — imagine the
least-practiced reader who will act on it, and write so that reader succeeds. If the
instruction only works for someone who already knows what you know, it is not yet
clear.

This pairs with progressive disclosure. Keep the top terse and plainly actionable;
push the rationale, edge cases, and vocabulary into a reference the reader opens only
if they need it. Depth below, action on top. (This skill is built that way: two
beats in the body, the methods here.)

## The reflexive self-check

Before you ship, read your output as the second reader would — someone holding only
your inputs and your output, not your head. Could they follow it and reach the same
place? Where the answer is no, the intent did not make it onto the page; fix that
spot.

Rank, do not flatten. What you are sure of and what you are guessing should not reach
the reader in the same confident voice — mark the difference, so they can tell solid
ground from soft.

## The warrant boundary

One thing this self-check brushes against is *warrant*: whether a claim rests on
something the reader can check, or only on your conviction. That matters — a claim
resting on conviction should be grounded in evidence or cut — but it is its own
discipline, owned elsewhere (in this corpus, the cite/source and readiness-claims
instruments). Surface Intent owns intent, reconnaissance, and legibility; it points
at the warrant seam and stops there, rather than restating a discipline that already
exists. That restraint is the skill's own Beat 1 applied to itself: do not add what
is already covered.
