# Acts — A

The acts: the alternatives genuinely available to the decision maker now. An act is something that can be committed to today; everything the world contributes afterward belongs to the states.

## Elicitation

Ask, in order:

1. "What could you actually do?" Take the alternatives as offered, then probe: "What option is missing from that list?" A decision presented as a binary usually is not one.
2. For each candidate act: "Could you commit to this today, with the resources and permissions you have?" Strike anything that fails.
3. "Is waiting an option?" Include "defer" as an act only when deferral is genuinely open — a real option with its own row in the consequence table, not a euphemism for one of the listed acts.
4. Check whether any listed act is secretly a sequence: a commitment now plus commitments later that depend on what is observed between them. Unpack those into stages (see Depth).

## Well-formedness checks

- **Feasibility**: every listed act can be initiated by the decision maker now. An act that requires someone else's uncoerced choice is not in A.
- **Distinctness**: no two acts lead to the same consequence in every state. If they do, they are one act written twice — merge them.
- **Defer is real**: if "defer" is listed, name what would be observed during the deferral and when the deferred choice comes due. Deferral without an observation is just a delayed version of the same decision, usually with worse terms.

## Failure modes

- **False dichotomies.** The act list arrives pre-narrowed to two, and the best alternative was excluded before analysis began. The unlisted act is the act-side twin of the unlisted state.
- **Acts conflated with outcomes.** "Succeed at the launch" is not an act; no one can commit to succeeding. When a candidate act embeds the world's cooperation, split it into the commitment (the act) and the cooperation (a state).
- **Compound acts hiding stages.** "Migrate to the new platform" may really be "run a pilot, then decide about full migration given the pilot's result." Evaluating the compound as one act loses the value of the decision point in the middle — usually the most valuable part.

## Depth

### Staging and reversibility

A multi-stage decision is representable in this framework as decide–observe–decide: an act now, an observation that refines what is known, then a dependent act. When a compound act unpacks this way, the first-stage alternatives should be compared on what they cost and what they let you observe, not only on their end states. Analysis of the observe step before it happens — asking what the observation could show and what you would do in each case — is how the value of a staged act gets computed.

Reversibility is the other axis: an act that can be unwound cheaply after an unfavorable observation is worth more than its consequence table alone shows, because part of its value is the exit.

### Option value

Some acts are valuable mostly for the acts they keep available. Under ambiguity and especially under unawareness — where the state list itself is suspect — this dominates: prefer commitments that stay small until the states reveal themselves, and treat "which options does this act preserve or destroy?" as a first-class attribute of every act.

When probabilities cannot be trusted at all, one usable anchor remains: how did relevantly similar past cases turn out? Ranking acts by their track record in analogous decisions is a legitimate rule when nothing licenses a distribution over S.

## Sources

- R. A. Howard, "Decision Analysis: Applied Decision Theory" (1966) — the discipline of formulating the alternatives before evaluating anything.
- H. Raiffa & R. Schlaifer, *Applied Statistical Decision Theory* (1961) — preposterior analysis: evaluating an observe-then-decide act by working through what each possible observation would lead you to do.
- I. Gilboa & D. Schmeidler, "Case-Based Decision Theory" (1995) — ranking acts by their outcomes in similar past cases when no probability over states is available.
- K. Binmore, *Rational Decisions* (2009) — the limits of Bayesian rationality in large worlds, and why small-step, revisable commitments are the defensible response.
