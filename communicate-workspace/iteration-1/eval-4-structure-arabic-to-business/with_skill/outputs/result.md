# Adapted Prose

Customers commit to a service when they trust it. We build that trust through consistency: the same outcome, delivered reliably, over time. The relationship holds as long as the consistency does.

---

# Structural Annotation

## Source tradition identified

The original uses parallel-elaborative rhetoric — the Semitic/Arabic organizational pattern documented in Kaplan (1966) and reframed by Connor (2002). Argument advances through coordinated restatement: "trust → commitment → trust → consistency → trust" forms a chain where each link revisits the previous claim with slight variation. Repetition is the persuasive mechanism, not a failure to economize. The thesis is not stated upfront; it is extracted by the reader from the accumulated weight of the pattern. This is a coherent, purposeful structure — it is not deficient.

## Target tradition identified

US business prose is linear-deductive and low-context. The reader expects: thesis first, mechanism or evidence below, conclusion last. Each sentence should add new information rather than restate the previous one. The audience will scan for the claim in the opening line; if it is not there, they will feel lost before they feel persuaded.

## Moves made

**1. Thesis moved to the opening sentence.**

Original: thesis emerges from the accumulated pattern across eight sentences.
Adapted: "Customers commit to a service when they trust it." — the claim is in the first nine words.

Cost: the reader no longer experiences the weight of the repeated pattern. The parallel-elaborative structure builds a felt conviction through accumulation; the linear version delivers a proposition. These are different cognitive experiences. The original asks the reader to participate in constructing the thesis; the adapted version hands it to them.

**2. Parallel-restatement chain compressed into a hierarchical evidence chain.**

Original: trust → commitment → trust → consistency → trust (eight sentences, each revisiting the chain).
Adapted: trust → consistency as mechanism → relationship as outcome (three sentences, each advancing rather than restating).

Cost: the accumulative persuasion is gone. The original's repetition is rhetorical force — it makes the reader feel the interdependence of trust and commitment through exposure. The adapted version asserts the mechanism directly but does not let it land through repetition.

**3. Implied causal link made explicit.**

Original: "Consistency, demonstrated repeatedly, builds trust" — the mechanism is asserted but the causal path is assumed.
Adapted: "the same outcome, delivered reliably, over time" — the definition of consistency is embedded as its own mechanism, making the causal logic self-contained for a low-context reader.

Cost: minor. The original's phrasing has more rhythm. The adapted version is more explicit but slightly more clinical.

**4. Circular closing converted to conditional closure.**

Original ends: "trust, once built through consistency, sustains the relationship our customers depend on" — circular, returns to the starting point.
Adapted ends: "The relationship holds as long as the consistency does" — conditional, forward-looking, states the maintenance requirement.

Cost: the original's circularity enacts its own argument (trust sustains trust sustains trust). The adapted version is more practical but loses that formal elegance.

## What does NOT change

Content, voice, vocabulary, and ideas are unchanged. The claim — that customer trust is built through demonstrated consistency and sustains the service relationship — is identical. Register (business, first-person plural, confident) is preserved.

## Option: preserve the original structure with a framing note

If the parallel-elaborative structure is the strongest part of this passage — and it may be, because the repetition enacts the argument — it can be preserved with a brief orientation for the US business audience:

> "The following passage builds its argument through restatement — each sentence returns to the same point from a slightly different angle. Read it through before judging whether the conclusion has landed."

Then the original, unchanged. Some US business readers will find this disorienting anyway; others will experience it as unusually persuasive precisely because of its unfamiliarity. The framing note makes the structure legible rather than invisible.
