# Bridge: *jeong* (정)

## Phase 1: Gaps identified

Three gaps operate here, not one.

**Conceptual lacuna (primary).** *Jeong* has no clean English lexical equivalent. "Affection," "attachment," "bond," and "love" each capture a fraction, but every one of them misfires somewhere. *Jeong* describes a relational tie that forms through accumulated shared time — often without either person deciding to form it — and that persists across conflict, distance, and even active dislike. You can have *jeong* for someone you would not choose as a friend. It is not sentiment; it is residue.

**Grammatical ontology.** Korean encodes *jeong* as something that forms *between* people, not something one person feels *toward* another. The directionality English requires ("I feel X for you") does not map cleanly onto a concept where the tie belongs to the relation, not to either party alone.

**Pragmatic convention.** In Korean relational culture, *jeong* is often most visible at the moment of separation or loss — you notice you had it when it is suddenly absent. It is not typically declared. Naming it directly can feel odd, like explaining a smell that is only recognizable in retrospect.

## Phase 2: Bilingual rendering

### In Korean

정 (*jeong*)

*"우리 사이에 정이 들었어."*
("*Jeong* has formed between us.")

### In English

There is no single English word. A working gloss:

*A bond that grows quietly from shared time — felt most clearly when it breaks.*

A fuller rendering in context:

*"Something between us had thickened over the years — not love exactly, not friendship either, but a kind of sediment that had built up without either of us choosing it. When she left, I realized what it was. There's a Korean word for it."*

### What Shifted

- **What English cannot preserve:** The relational grammar. Korean places *jeong* in the space between people ("정이 들었다" — "*jeong* has accumulated," passive and agentless). English must assign a subject: "I feel attached," "we were close." The feeling becomes owned rather than shared. The ontological weight shifts from the relation to one of the parties.

- **Where English requires explicit content the Korean leaves implicit:** Korean speakers rarely need to explain what *jeong* is — the word carries the whole concept. English rendering requires circumlocution: "something between us," "accumulated over time," "not chosen." Three phrases to replace one syllable.

- **What is lost entirely:** The ambivalence. *Jeong* can coexist with dislike, with irritation, even with a wish the other person were gone. English words for connection ("love," "affection," "attachment") carry positive valence by default. None of them accommodates the person who says, "I can't stand him, but I'd miss him if he left."

- **What survives the translation:** The temporal quality — that *jeong* is accumulated, not declared — comes through in English with the right framing. "Years of small moments" or "sediment" gets close. The thing that resists is not the duration but the passivity: *jeong* happens to you; English tends to make you the agent.

## Phase 3: Direct English (for low-context readers)

If the context demands plain English without gloss or circumlocution:

### Direct English

*A quiet bond that builds without being chosen — often noticed only when it's gone.*

### What This Version Sacrifices

The relational grammar is fully lost. "A bond" makes it a noun one party holds; the Korean has it living between people with no clear possessor. The ambivalence — the possibility of *jeong* toward someone you dislike — disappears. And the passivity weakens: "builds without being chosen" still implies an agent; *jeong* implies none. This version is comprehensible to a reader who has never heard the word. It is not equivalent to the word.
