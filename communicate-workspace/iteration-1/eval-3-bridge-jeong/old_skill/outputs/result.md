# Bridge: *Jeong* (정) → English

**Gap type:** Conceptual lacuna (Type 3) with pragmatic convention gap (Type 4).
**Source tradition:** Korean / High-Context East Asian.

---

### In Korean

정 (*jeong*)

[User-provided term. The full phrase in context might be: "우리 사이에 정이 들었어" — "jeong has formed between us" — or simply invoking the word as a standalone concept.]

### In English

There is no single English word for *jeong*. The closest cluster of approximations: "deep affective bond," "attachment that accumulates over time," "the feeling of being bound to someone through shared life." None of these is *jeong*.

If you need a working English rendering in a sentence: "There's a bond between us that has grown through everything we've been through together — not exactly love, not exactly friendship, something that just accumulated and now holds."

---

### What Shifted

- **The concept has no English equivalent.** English has "love," "affection," "attachment," "fondness," "closeness" — *jeong* is none of these, and is not the sum of them. It is a distinct category. Any English rendering is an approximation that loses the category itself.

- **The temporal dimension.** *Jeong* accumulates passively over time and through shared experience — it is not chosen or declared, it forms the way sediment forms. English "bond" or "attachment" can be chosen; *jeong* is something that happens to you through the fact of having lived alongside someone. The English rendering has to add words ("grown through everything," "accumulated") to approximate what *jeong* encodes in a single syllable.

- **The relational scope.** *Jeong* can form with people you do not particularly like, with a place, even with a habit or object — it tracks co-presence and shared experience, not positive feeling. English "love" and "affection" imply positive valence; *jeong* does not. A Korean speaker might say "I have *jeong* with this difficult person I've worked beside for twenty years" — not meaning "I like them," but meaning "we are now bound in a way that would hurt to cut." English struggles to hold this.

- **The high-context embedding.** In Korean communication, *jeong* is often invoked without explanation — the listener knows the full weight of what is being said. Rendering it in English requires unpacking: the single word expands into a clause or a paragraph. The explicitness required by English low-context norms is itself a form of loss — it makes visible what the Korean left efficiently, elegantly implied.

- **What survives.** The emotional reality survives — readers of the English approximation will recognize the feeling being described. What does not survive is the precision: the single word that names the category, the compactness, and the cultural understanding that *jeong* is a force operating at the level of social fabric rather than individual choice.

### Direct English (optional)

"We've been through enough together that cutting ties would cost us both something real — not love exactly, but something that has grown into its own weight."

### What This Version Sacrifices

This version converts *jeong* into its effects (the cost of cutting ties) rather than naming the bond itself. It is emotionally legible to an English reader but loses the ontological point: *jeong* names a thing that exists, not just a feeling about the prospect of loss. The original Korean names the bond; this English version describes what the bond would feel like if broken. That is a fundamentally different move — describing the shadow of a concept rather than the concept itself.
