## Spanish

### Ser/Estar Distinction

Two verbs for "to be" encoding whether a quality is essential or contingent.

| Spanish | English | What It Encodes |
|---|---|---|
| "Es inteligente" (ser) | "Is intelligent" | Intelligence as essential quality — part of who this person is |
| "Esta cansada" (estar) | "Is tired" | Tiredness as transient state — temporary condition, not identity |
| "Es triste" (ser) | "Is sad" | Sadness as defining characteristic — a melancholic person |
| "Esta triste" (estar) | "Is sad" | Sadness as current state — sad right now, not by nature |

**Annotation example:**
> English collapses "es triste" and "esta triste" into "is sad." The original distinguishes sadness-as-identity from sadness-as-state — the speaker was encoding whether this quality defines the person or merely visits them. The English version loses this temporal/ontological distinction.

### Tener + Emotional State

Emotions as possessions rather than identity states.

| Spanish | Literal English | Natural English | What Shifts |
|---|---|---|---|
| "Tengo miedo" | "I have fear" | "I am afraid" | Self/emotion boundary: carrier vs. identity |
| "Tengo hambre" | "I have hunger" | "I am hungry" | Bodily state externalized vs. internalized |
| "Tengo frio" | "I have cold" | "I am cold" | Temperature as carried experience vs. being |
| "Tengo razon" | "I have reason" | "I am right" | Rightness as something held vs. something inhabited |

**Annotation example:**
> "Tengo miedo" encodes fear as something the speaker carries — an external weight, not an internal transformation. "I am afraid" collapses the self into the emotion; the Spanish construction keeps them separate. The estar form provides a natural linguistic distancing tool for emotion regulation that English does not offer grammatically (Holmes et al., 2023).

### Subjunctive as Epistemic Marker

The subjunctive mood in Spanish marks uncertainty, desire, doubt, and subjective judgment — grammatical territory English navigates through auxiliary verbs and adverbs.

| Spanish | English | What It Encodes |
|---|---|---|
| "Espero que venga" (subjunctive) | "I hope they come" | Grammatical marker of uncertainty — the coming is not established fact |
| "Dudo que sea verdad" (subjunctive) | "I doubt it's true" | Doubt encoded in verb morphology, not just the word "doubt" |
| "Es importante que estudies" (subjunctive) | "It's important that you study" | The studying is desired/required, not factual — mood encodes this |

**Annotation example:**
> Spanish grammatically marks this statement as existing in the domain of desire/uncertainty through the subjunctive mood. English must rely on context and auxiliary words to signal the same epistemic status. The Spanish version carries its uncertainty in its verb form — a structural commitment to epistemological honesty that English can only approximate lexically.

## Japanese

### Particles (Wa/Ga)

Topic marker *wa* and subject marker *ga* encode information structure and emphasis.

| Japanese | Function | What It Encodes |
|---|---|---|
| "Watashi **wa** gakusei desu" | Topic marker | "As for me, [I am] a student" — the speaker is the topic of discussion |
| "Watashi **ga** gakusei desu" | Subject/focus marker | "*I* am the student" — emphasis on the speaker being the one (not someone else) |

**Annotation example:**
> The wa/ga distinction encodes whether the speaker is setting a topic for discussion or asserting identity against alternatives. English word order and stress can approximate this ("I am a student" vs. "*I'm* the student"), but the distinction is grammatical in Japanese and prosodic in English — a fundamentally different level of encoding.

### Sentence-Final Particles

Particles that encode the speaker's epistemic and relational stance.

| Particle | Function | Relational Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| *ne* | Seeks confirmation/shared experience | "We both know this, right?" — positions knowledge as communal |
| *yo* | Asserts information | "I'm telling you this" — positions the speaker as holding knowledge the listener lacks |
| *kana* | Expresses doubt/wonder | "I wonder..." — softens the claim, invites collaborative thinking |
| *na* | Self-directed reflection | "Hmm, it's like this..." — thinking aloud, not addressing the listener directly |
| *yone* | Combined confirmation + assertion | "It is this way, and we both feel it, right?" — complex relational positioning |

**Annotation example:**
> The speaker's use of *ne* positions this observation as shared — inviting agreement rather than asserting fact. English has no single-word equivalent; "right?" or "don't you think?" are approximate but carry different connotations (the tag question can sound uncertain or aggressive depending on context, while *ne* is relationally warm). The particle encodes the speaker's relationship to both the claim and the listener in a single syllable.

### Honorifics as Relational Positioning

Japanese honorific system (*keigo*) encodes the speaker's social relationship to the subject and listener.

| Level | Function | What It Encodes |
|---|---|---|
| *sonkeigo* (respectful) | Elevates the subject | The speaker positions the subject as higher — a statement about relationship, not just politeness |
| *kenjougo* (humble) | Lowers the speaker | The speaker positions themselves as lower — humility as relational positioning |
| *teineigo* (polite) | Neutral distance | The speaker maintains formal distance — neither elevating nor lowering |

**Annotation example:**
> The honorific level chosen encodes the speaker's understood social relationship with the listener. This is not mere politeness — it is relational positioning that structures the entire utterance. English has no grammatical equivalent; it approximates through word choice, register, and address forms, but these are stylistic rather than structural.

## Arabic

### Verbal Morphology

Arabic verb forms encode patterns of agency, causation, and reciprocity.

| Form | Function | What It Encodes |
|---|---|---|
| Form I (base) | Basic action | Simple agency — someone does something |
| Form II (intensive) | Intensification/causation | Increased force or causing another to do the action |
| Form V (reflexive of II) | Reflexive/gradual | The action happening to or within the self, often gradually |
| Form VI (reciprocal) | Mutual action | Both parties acting on each other simultaneously |
| Agentless constructions | Action without named agent | Cause understood as natural, divine, or systemic — not individual |

**Annotation example:**
> The Arabic agentless construction here encodes an action without a human initiator. The absence of a named agent is not passive voice in the English sense — it is a semantic choice indicating that the cause is understood as beyond individual human agency. English "it was done" implies a hidden human actor; the Arabic form implies no human actor exists for this action.

### Parallel Elaboration

Argument through accumulation, repetition, and stylistic intensification.

**Pattern:** State a point. Restate with variation. Elaborate with a parallel construction. Build the reader's conviction through the weight of repeated, layered assertion.

**Annotation example:**
> The parallel structure here is persuasion through accumulation — meaning builds as the reader encounters the same claim from multiple angles. The English linear version compresses this into a single assertion with supporting evidence. The reader processes faster but does not feel the weight of the repeated pattern. The accumulative persuasion is replaced by hierarchical evidence.

## French

### Imparfait/Passe Compose Distinction

Two past tenses encoding different relationships to past experience.

| French | English | What It Encodes |
|---|---|---|
| "Je mangeais" (imparfait) | "I was eating" / "I used to eat" | Ongoing, habitual, or background past — the action as experienced from within |
| "J'ai mange" (passe compose) | "I ate" | Completed past — the action as a finished event viewed from outside |

**Annotation example:**
> French distinguishes between experiencing a past action from within its duration (imparfait) and viewing it as a completed event (passe compose). English approximates this with "was eating" vs. "ate," but the French distinction carries more: imparfait locates the reader inside the experience; passe compose steps outside it. This is a narrative perspective tool — not just a tense marker.

### Subordinate Clause Accumulation

French academic prose builds epistemic rigor through layered qualification.

**Pattern:** Main clause, qualified by subordinate clause, further qualified by relative clause, contextualized by conditional clause — all before the sentence resolves.

**Annotation example:**
> The accumulation of subordinate clauses here signals epistemic care in the French tradition — the writer demonstrates awareness of qualifications, conditions, and counter-possibilities before committing to the main assertion. In English, this reads as "overly complex." In French academic convention, it reads as rigorous. The direct English version sacrifices the demonstration of intellectual thoroughness.

### Epistemic Structures

French encodes intellectual distance and philosophical commitment through grammatical and rhetorical patterns.

| Pattern | Function | What It Encodes |
|---|---|---|
| Conditional mood for hypothesis | "Ce serait..." (it would be...) | Intellectual caution — the writer signals this is a proposition, not a fact |
| Impersonal "on" | "On pourrait dire..." (one could say...) | Generalization that includes the speaker without centering them |
| Dialectical structure | Thesis, antithesis, synthesis | Intellectual commitment to holding contradiction before resolution |

## High-Context East Asian

### Implied Subject

Japanese, Chinese, and Korean frequently omit the grammatical subject when it can be inferred from context.

**Annotation example:**
> The original omits the subject entirely — the reader is trusted to infer who acts from context. Adding "I" or "they" in English makes the agency explicit but removes the high-context quality of the original, where meaning is distributed between text and shared understanding. The English version is more explicit but less trusting of the reader.

### Face-Saving Indirection

Refusal, disagreement, and negative evaluation communicated through what is not said or through softened formulation.

| What Is Said | What It Communicates | The Indirection |
|---|---|---|
| "That would be difficult" | "No" | Refusal without confrontation |
| "I will consider it" | "Probably not" | Delay as soft rejection |
| "There may be some challenges" | "This will not work" | Understatement as face preservation |
| Silence or topic change | Disagreement | Absence of agreement signals dissent |

**Annotation example:**
> The indirection here is not evasion — it is a relational strategy that preserves the listener's face while communicating a clear message to anyone within the cultural context. The direct English version ("No, that won't work") communicates the same propositional content but destroys the relational work the original performs. The choice between versions is a choice between semantic efficiency and relational care.

## Annotation Principles

### Name the Ontological Difference

Every annotation explains **why** the languages differ at the level of worldview, not just **that** they differ at the level of grammar.

| Avoid | Use |
|---|---|
| "Spanish uses tener for emotions" | "Spanish encodes emotions as carried possessions — the self holds the feeling rather than becoming it" |
| "Japanese hedges more" | "Japanese particles encode the speaker's epistemic relationship to the claim — marking whether knowledge is shared, asserted, or uncertain" |
| "Arabic is more repetitive" | "Arabic builds conviction through accumulated restatement — meaning emerges from the pattern, not from a single assertion" |
| "French is more formal" | "French subordination signals epistemic rigor — the writer demonstrates awareness of qualifications before committing" |

### Preserve the Source Logic

When the L1 pattern encodes precision that English lacks, the annotation should name what English loses — not frame the L1 pattern as unusual.

### Let the Writer Choose

Present both versions and annotate the differences. The writer decides which rendering serves their purpose. The skill annotates; it does not prescribe.
