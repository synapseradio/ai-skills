# Frame Phase — Extended Guidance

Detailed guidance for Phase 1 of the writing skill. The Frame phase establishes the generative foundation: necessity, purpose, position, and audience/tradition context. Without a strong Frame, composition produces content instead of writing.

---

## Necessity Questions

Necessity is the first question and the generative force. "Why must this be written?" is not a planning step -- it is the engine that shapes all subsequent decisions. Writing driven by necessity produces different work than writing driven by instruction.

### Probing Necessity

Ask these questions to help the writer surface what compels the piece to exist:

- **What would be lost if this were not written?** If nothing would be lost, the piece may not need to exist -- or the writer has not yet found the necessity. Help them find it before proceeding.

- **Whose experience does this preserve?** Poniatowska built her body of work on recording voices official history erases. Ngugi wrote on toilet paper in prison because the story had to exist even when the state tried to silence it. Necessity often lives in preservation.

- **What does this piece insist upon?** Achebe framed writing as civilizational restoration -- the writer's job is to restore what colonialism stole. Al-Shaykh began writing "as a way of being sure of her own existence." Necessity can be existential, political, or personal -- but it must be nameable.

- **Is this writing-as-discovery or writing-as-transmission?** If the writer already knows exactly what they want to say, the necessity may be communicative. If the writer has a felt need that does not yet have a form, the necessity is generative -- and the Frame phase should leave room for what drafting will reveal.

### When Necessity Is Not Yet Clear

Do not fabricate necessity. If the writer cannot articulate why this must be written, that is diagnostic information. Possible responses:

- The piece may not be ready to be written yet. Acknowledge this without judgment.
- The necessity may emerge through exploratory drafting. Suggest a short exploratory draft (Phase 3) with the understanding that the Frame will be revisited in Discover (Phase 4).
- The writer may know the necessity but not how to name it. Ask: "What feeling or conviction prompted this? What event, conversation, or realization made this feel urgent?"

Ernaux's method -- "an ethnology of myself" -- treats personal experience as data worth examining. The necessity does not need to be dramatic. It needs to be real.

## Position Questions

Position is mandatory in the Frame phase. Writing comes from somewhere, and that somewhere shapes what can be said, what authority grounds it, and what remains invisible.

### The Three-Question Framework

Every writer must answer these before composition begins:

**1. "What is my relationship to this subject?"**

The writer's proximity to the subject determines what kind of knowledge they bring. Direct experience, professional expertise, scholarly study, secondhand knowledge, and outsider observation are all valid positions -- but they enable and constrain different claims.

Questions to surface relationship:

- Have I lived this, studied it, observed it, or heard about it?
- Am I inside the community this affects, adjacent to it, or outside it?
- What form of contact with this subject do I have?

**2. "What can I legitimately claim?"**

Based on the relationship identified above, what authority does the writer actually hold? Luiselli insists on "recognizing the limitations of the position that we occupy in society." This is not self-deprecation -- it is calibration.

Questions to surface legitimate claims:

- What have I witnessed firsthand?
- What have I verified through evidence or research?
- What am I inferring, and from what basis?
- Where does my authority end and speculation begin?

**3. "What am I unable to see from where I stand?"**

Every position has blind spots. The point is not to eliminate them -- that is impossible -- but to name them so the reader can calibrate.

Questions to surface blind spots:

- What perspectives on this subject would I never encounter from my position?
- What would someone from a different position say I am missing?
- What assumptions does my position make that I have not examined?

### Position Is Not a Disclaimer

Do not render positional acknowledgment as a throat-clearing paragraph ("As a [identity], I acknowledge..."). Position is a structural decision:

- It shapes what the writing can do (firsthand account vs. analytical overview vs. advocacy)
- It shapes what evidence is available (lived experience vs. research vs. testimony)
- It shapes what voice is appropriate (witness vs. expert vs. participant)

Ngugi's choice to write in Gikuyu was a positional commitment, not a style preference. The position determines the writing, not the other way around.

## Tradition Identification

Before structural decisions in Phase 2, identify the rhetorical tradition the writer is working in or for. This is loaded in Phase 0 (Orient) but the Frame phase must account for it when defining audience expectations.

### How to Identify the Operating Tradition

Ask:

- **What language does the writer think in?** Writers whose first language is not English may carry organizational logic from their L1 tradition (Arabic parallel-restatement, French dialectical construction, Japanese indirect development).
- **What audience is this for?** The target audience's tradition matters as much as the writer's. French academic readers expect dialectical structure. American business readers expect direct thesis-first structure. Japanese readers may expect indirection and implication.
- **What genre does this operate in?** Academic writing, journalism, literary nonfiction, business communication, and personal essay each carry tradition-specific conventions that may override the writer's native tradition.

### Tradition Interacts with Frame

The identified tradition modifies how Frame outputs are used:

- **Necessity** may look different across traditions. Arabic tradition may frame necessity through communal storytelling obligation. French tradition may frame it through intellectual contribution to a problem (*problematique*). Japanese tradition may frame it through *mondai-ishiki* (problem-consciousness).
- **Position** conventions vary. Anglo-American academic writing claims objectivity. French writing demonstrates intellectual rigor through dialectical positioning. Japanese writing positions through indirection and deference.
- **Purpose** articulation differs. Some traditions expect explicit thesis statements; others build toward implied conclusions.

For full tradition taxonomy and structural signatures, load `tradition.md`.

## Audience Analysis

The final Frame element: who receives this, and what do they bring?

### Core Questions

- **What does the audience already know?** Map their existing knowledge to identify what can be assumed and what must be established. Load calibrate for depth assessment.

- **What rhetorical conventions does the audience expect?** This is the cultural dimension of calibration. An American business reader expects directness and brevity. A French academic reader expects thorough intellectual scaffolding. A Japanese reader may expect the writer to leave space for the reader's own inference.

- **What is the audience's relationship to this subject?** Are they peers, novices, skeptics, allies, decision-makers? The relationship shapes what the writing asks of them.

- **What does the writer want the audience to do, feel, or understand?** This connects back to purpose. The audience analysis should clarify whether the purpose is achievable with this audience -- and if not, whether the purpose or the audience needs to shift.

### Audience and Tradition Together

When the writer's tradition differs from the audience's tradition, Flag this explicitly. The Frame output should note:

- "Writer operates in [tradition A]; audience expects [tradition B]"
- "This mismatch may require structural adaptation in Phase 2 or bridging in Phase 5"
- "Consider whether to adapt to the audience's tradition or to keep the writer's tradition and bridge explicitly"

This decision -- adapt or bridge -- is one of the most consequential in the Frame phase. It is not a default; it requires the writer's input.
