# Across Languages

Self-interrogation reference. Load before adapting prose across rhetorical traditions, before bridging a thought that resists target-language expression, before "correcting" L1 grammar carrying through L2 prose. Questions replace the static taxonomy.

## Methodological note

Rhetorical traditions are documented patterns. They are not deterministic rules. Kaplan's 1966 "doodles" — English linear, Semitic parallel, Asian spiral, Romance digressive — has been critiqued for stereotyping (Kubota 1999). Connor herself moved from "contrastive rhetoric" to "intercultural rhetoric" in 2002. Treat the patterns below as priors to interrogate, not labels to apply. An individual writer is a person, not a statistic.

## Self-interrogation questions

### Is this prose using a non-default organizational logic?

The model's default is linear-deductive: thesis first, evidence below, conclusion last. That is one tradition, not the universal one. Do not silently normalize toward it.

**Parallel-elaborative** (Arabic, Semitic). Argument advances through coordinated restatement. The thesis emerges from the accumulated pattern. Repetition is persuasive force, not redundancy. A paragraph saying X, then X with variation, then X in a parallel construction is doing the argument — not failing to remember it has been said.

**Dialectical** (French academic; the *dissertation*). Thesis → antithesis → synthesis. The writer earns a position by inhabiting its opposite. A passage arguing against itself before resolving *is* the argument; do not flatten to thesis-first.

**Indirect-implicative** (Japanese; *ki-shō-ten-ketsu*). Introduce, develop, twist, integrate. The conclusion is often implied. An essay that never states its thesis but arranges evidence so the thesis is unmistakable: the unstated thesis is the strongest claim.

**Accumulative-scaffolded** (German academic). Define everything, qualify exhaustively, then commit. The scaffolding *is* the rigor. Compression destroys what was being demonstrated.

**Spiral/recursive** (associated with Latin American written prose drawing on oral tradition; Connor 1996 cautions against essentialism here). Themes return at different angles; narrative carries argumentative weight. *Testimonio* (Menchú; Poniatowska) and the narrative cycles in García Márquez are anchor cases. An argument that returns to its central image three times, each turn deepening, is method, not repetition.

When prose feels "foreign," name the tradition before adjusting. Adaptation without naming is silent normalization.

### Is this hedging epistemic calibration or habitual cushioning?

Cut habitual cushioning. Preserve epistemic calibration.

- "It's worth noting," "it's important to note," "in essence" — habitual cushioning. Cut.
- "May," "might," "perhaps" attached to a claim with genuinely uncertain warrant — calibration. Preserve.
- Japanese sentence-final particles (*ne*, *yo*, *kana*) — calibration English cannot replicate grammatically. Do not strip.
- French/German subordinate-clause accumulation bounding a claim — calibration. Do not flatten to one clause.

### Is L1 grammar carrying through L2 prose?

When it does, ask what the L1 pattern encodes that L2 cannot.

**Spanish ser vs estar.** Two verbs for "to be." *Es triste* = sadness as identity. *Está triste* = sadness as state. English collapses both to "is sad." If the writer means the temporal distinction, English needs the extra word: "feels sad right now," "is a sad person."

**Spanish tener + emotion.** *Tengo miedo* = "I have fear." Emotion is carried, not inhabited. "I am afraid" merges self and feeling. Do not "correct" *tengo miedo* without naming the loss.

**Japanese sentence-final particles.** One syllable encodes epistemic and relational stance. *Ne* proposes shared knowledge. *Yo* asserts. *Kana* mitigates. English needs a separate clause or tag question; the particle is structurally lost.

**Japanese implied subject.** The reader infers who acts from context. Adding "I" or "they" makes action explicit but removes the high-context quality. Choose explicitness only when needed.

**Arabic agentless construction.** A verb without an explicit agent may encode natural or systemic causation. English passive "it was done" implies a hidden human; the Arabic does not.

**French imparfait.** *Je mangeais* = experienced from within duration. *J'ai mangé* = viewed from outside as completed. "Was eating" / "ate" approximates but loses precision.

**German modal particles.** *Doch*, *ja*, *eben*, *halt*, *mal* carry contextual and relational weight. *Komm doch mit* = "come along [you know you want to]." English cannot say it in one word.

On seeing one of these patterns, ask: what does the writer mean? If they mean what the L1 encodes, the L2 needs annotation, not correction.

### Does this concept have a clean target-language equivalent?

Some do not. Do not manufacture false equivalents.

- *Saudade* (Portuguese): a present-tense relationship with absence. Not nostalgia. Not grief. Longing that knows return may be impossible.
- *Hiraeth* (Welsh): longing for a place, sometimes one never visited or no longer existing. The place is the focus, not the relationship.
- *Wabi-sabi* (Japanese): beauty located in impermanence and incompleteness. Not "rustic charm."
- *Dor* (Romanian): longing tinged with grief. Heavier than *saudade*.
- *Jugaad* (Hindi): improvised, resource-constrained workaround that gets the job done. Contested in usage.

When no clean mapping exists, say so. Offer a gloss. Do not pick the nearest English word and hope.

### What does target-language rendering destroy? Is the loss worth the gain?

Every cross-tradition adaptation costs something. Name the cost.

- Arabic parallel-restatement → hierarchical evidence. Lost: the felt weight of repeated pattern; the reader's experience of extracting the thesis.
- French dialectical → thesis-first. Lost: the demonstration of inhabiting the counter-position.
- Japanese implied → explicit. Lost: reader participation; the dignity of implication.
- German scaffolded → compressed. Lost: the intellectual landscape visible before commitment.
- Spiral → linear. Lost: recursive depth; ecosystem framing.

If the cost matters, offer the source structure with a framing note for the target audience as a third option. Adaptation is not the only response.

### Is the gap I'm about to fill doing rhetorical work?

Some silences are structural.

- Intentional omission sharpens focus.
- Strategic silence lets the reader complete the thought. The unsaid lands harder than the said.
- High-context traditions distribute meaning between text and shared knowledge. Over-explaining insults the reader.

Before adding content, ask: is this gap a flaw or a feature? If feature, do not fill.

## NEVER

- Never default to Anglo-American norms as the unmarked baseline. Name tradition-specific choices as tradition-specific.
- Never treat L1 grammatical carry-over as error. Diagnose what the pattern encodes before treating it as a problem.
- Never strip all hedging. Apply the calibration-vs-cushioning test per instance.
- Never produce false equivalents for concepts that lack clean mappings. Say so; offer a gloss.
- Never assume statistical tendencies determine an individual writer's choices.
- Never adapt structure silently. Annotate every move; offer the source structure as an option.
