# Compose from a Blank Page

Read this when the user starts from nothing: no draft, no outline, often only a felt need. Establish four things before any structural decision: necessity, position, tradition, audience. Skipping them produces content instead of writing.

## Necessity

Ask why this must be written. Writing driven by necessity produces different work than writing driven by instruction.

- What would be lost if this were not written? When the answer says nothing, the piece may not need to exist yet; saying so helps more than fabricating a reason.
- Whose experience does this preserve? Poniatowska recorded voices official history erases. Ngugi wrote on toilet paper in prison because the story had to exist even when the state tried to silence it.
- What does this piece insist upon? Necessity can be existential, political, or personal, and it must be nameable.
- Does the writer already know what they want to say, or does a felt need still lack a form? Transmission calls for framing now. Discovery calls for an exploratory draft first; revisit these questions after it reveals what the piece wants to say.

When the user cannot articulate necessity, treat that as information rather than failure. Ask what event, conversation, or realization made this feel urgent. Ernaux treated her own experience as data worth examining: necessity needs no drama, only existence.

## Position

Writing comes from somewhere, and that somewhere decides what the piece can claim. [voice](./voice.md) holds three questions to ask before composing: what relationship the writer has to this subject, what they can legitimately claim from it, and what they cannot see from where they stand.

Treat the answers as structural decisions. Position picks the stance (witness, expert, participant), the available evidence (lived experience, research, testimony), and the fitting voice. Ngugi's choice to write in Gikuyu committed the work, not the styling. Never render position as a throat-clearing paragraph ("As a [identity], I acknowledge...").

## Tradition

Identify the rhetorical tradition the piece will operate in before choosing its structure. Ask what language the writer thinks in, what tradition the audience expects, and what conventions the genre imposes over both.

When the writer's tradition and the audience's differ, flag it and put one question to the user: adapt to the audience's tradition, or keep the writer's and bridge explicitly? [across-languages](./across-languages.md) holds per-tradition patterns and what each adaptation costs.

## Audience

Map what the audience already knows ([calibrate](./calibrate.md) helps), what conventions they expect, and their relationship to the subject: peers, novices, skeptics, allies, decision-makers. Then name what the writer wants them to do, feel, or understand.

When that purpose cannot land with this audience, say so. Either the purpose or the audience shifts, and the user decides which.
