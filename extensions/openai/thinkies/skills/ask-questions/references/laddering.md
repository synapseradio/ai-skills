# Climb to What Matters

Reach the value under a stated preference by asking what matters about each answer.

## Diagnostic Question

Ask: Do I have what they prefer, but not why it matters to them?
The stated preference is the bottom rung. The value it serves sits several answers up.

This climbs *up*, from a preference to the value under it — distinct from the other ladders. Building a ladder composes an inquiry, rung by rung, toward a driving question. Climbing down walks a conclusion *down* to the observable data it stands on. Reach here only when you have what they prefer and want why it matters.

The climb needs a responsive answerer — one rung per turn, the live mode named in the core. Against a document, or when composing in one pass, either switch to [climb-down](./climb-down.md) or [probe](./probe.md), or compose the anticipated rungs with a note on where each branch depends on the answer before it.

## Instructions

1. **Ask what matters about the answer you just got.** Then ask it again of the new answer, and again. Each pass climbs one rung from attribute toward consequence toward value.

2. **Let the chain decide where to stop.** An answer is terminal when it names something valued for itself — an end-state, an identity, a feeling that serves nothing further. If the last answer still names a means to something else, there's another rung; the climb ends at a terminal answer, not at your patience.

3. **Vary the phrasing each rung.** Never repeat the question verbatim. "What does that give you?", "What would having that mean?", "What's important about that?" all climb the same rung in different words.

4. **Watch for fatigue.** The repetition wears people down. When their answers shorten or their tone flattens, respect their limits — pause the climb, or reroute: probe one element, climb down to a concrete instance, and return to the climb later if it still matters.

5. **Watch for performed rationality.** Under repeated probing, people construct justifications to look reasonable rather than report what actually drives them. When answers turn abstract or suspiciously tidy, anchor to a concrete past instance: "When did that last happen? Walk me through it." After about three consecutive rungs, anchor to a concrete instance before climbing again — beyond that, answers drift toward constructed justification.

6. **Unblock a stalled climb with the move that fits the stall.** Vague answers → evoke the situation it happens in. They can't say why it matters → ask what lacking it would be like, or ladder the negative: why do they NOT want the alternatives? Sensitive ground → shift to third person. Abstract drift → contrast with an earlier time in their life. You suspect you've misread them → repeat back what they said and ask for correction.

7. **Soften "why" on sensitive ground.** A bare "why" reads as a demand to justify, and people defend instead of explain. Reach for "what led to that?" or "how did that come about?" — same information, none of the accusation.

8. **Ask sideways when the direct ask would corrupt.** When asking for the value outright would get a performed answer, ask a concrete proxy — a story, a choice they made, a trade-off they accepted — and infer the value from it. What you want to know and what you ask are different things.

9. **Hand back the chain.** When the climb terminates, report the whole chain — preference, consequences, value — and what it licenses next, per the core's contract.

## Questions

- What matters to them about the last answer?
- Has the chain stopped moving on its own, or have I just stopped pushing?
- Am I about to repeat a phrasing verbatim?
- Are the answers turning abstract — do I need a concrete past instance?
- If the climb has stalled, which unblocking move fits this stall?

## Quality Criteria

When the climb is doing its work:

- [ ] Each rung asked what mattered about the previous answer, in fresh words.
- [ ] The climb ended where the chain resisted a further "why," not where patience ran out.
- [ ] Abstract or tidy answers were anchored to concrete past instances.
- [ ] Signs of fatigue were respected rather than pushed through.
- [ ] Sensitive ground got "what led to that?" or third-person phrasing, never a bare "why."
