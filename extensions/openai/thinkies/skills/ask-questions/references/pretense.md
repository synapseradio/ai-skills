# Audit the Pretending Question

Find the question that treats the thing as simpler than it is, and make it pay its debts.

## Diagnostic Question

Ask: Which question in this inquiry is pretending — treating the object as simpler than it is — and where is its audit?

## Instructions

1. **Expect a pretending question.** Most inquiries contain one: a question that deliberately treats the thing as simpler than it is — "what does this look like if it behaves simply?" That question is often the most productive rung on the ladder, and it is licensed only with its audits. Some inquiries genuinely contain none — "none found" is a valid outcome; say what you searched and found clean.

2. **Classify the simplification.** Is it a scaffold you'll remove once the real structure stands, or a claim that the removed parts don't matter? The two owe different debts: a scaffold owes a removal plan, a claim owes a defense of what it discarded. A simplification that is both owes both.

3. **Hunt tigers, not mice.** Ask where the simple picture is importantly wrong — the error that would change the conclusion, not the small deviation that wouldn't. Then name which later question is responsible for catching it. When no question in hand owns that job, author the missing audit question yourself — composing questions is this skill's trade. A pretense whose audit question can't be authored yet returns as a flagged finding.

4. **Find the simplest case that breaks it.** Look for the smallest, plainest situation where the simple picture fails. When you find one, ask which hidden assumption it exposed — and make the question you hand back state that assumption explicitly. Surfacing the assumption is your deliverable; repairing the claim belongs to its author.

5. **Never define the breaking case away.** Redrawing the boundaries so the counterexample no longer counts protects the fiction instead of auditing it. The breaking case is information; absorb it into the claim.

## Questions

- Which question here is pretending?
- Is the simplification a scaffold we'll remove, or a claim that the removed parts don't matter?
- Where is the simple picture importantly wrong, and which later question is responsible for catching it?
- What's the simplest case that breaks it, and which hidden assumption did it expose?
- Does the question you hand back state that assumption out loud, or has the breaking case been defined away?

## Quality Criteria

When the audit is doing its work:

- [ ] The pretending question was located, or its absence declared after a real search.
- [ ] The simplification was classified — scaffold or claim — and its debt named accordingly.
- [ ] The hunt targeted errors that would change the conclusion, not trivial deviations.
- [ ] A specific later question — found or authored — owns the job of catching the pretense's failure.
- [ ] Breaking cases were absorbed into the claim, never defined away.
