# Voice

Find and maintain consistent authorial voice across different contexts, including the writer's positional relationship to their subject.

## Diagnostic Question

Ask: Does this sound like one person wrote it? Does that person speak from a stated place?
Where does the sensibility shift - casual to formal, certain to hesitant - without clear purpose? Is there a coherent authorial identity, or does the writing sound generic and interchangeable? Who speaks, and from what relationship to this subject? What can they legitimately claim? What are they unable to see from where they stand?

## Instructions

1. **Identify the voice's character** - Examine what perspective animates the writing: formality or casualness, directness or indirection, certainty or qualification. Notice sentence-level choices that reveal attitude, word selection that signals sensibility.

2. **Establish core attributes** - Define what remains stable across contexts: the relationship with readers, assumptions about their needs, tone toward subject matter. Distinguish essential voice markers from adaptable surface features.

3. **Analyze for authenticity** - Examine sentence-level patterns to detect whether they sound like natural thought or constructed performance. Voice that's trying to communicate differs from voice that's trying to impress.

4. **Track consistency** - Compare sections to verify they sound like the same person, noting where voice shifts with purpose versus where it drifts without reason. Readers should never wonder if different authors took over.

5. **Adapt without losing identity** - Voice modulates for context while remaining recognizable, the way a person sounds like themselves across different situations. Preserve core sensibility while adjusting surface features.

6. **Analyze natural cadence** - Identify the rhythm this voice exhibits: long sentences building through accumulation or short sentences punching with clarity. Preserve the pace that emerges rather than imposing external rules.

7. **Notice what the voice avoids** - Pay attention to patterns of absence that define character through constraint. What this voice doesn't do shapes identity as much as what it does.

### Authorial Stance

Voice answers "how does the writer sound?" Authorial stance answers "where does the writer stand?" A coherent voice without a stated position defaults to a generic, unmarked register - an invisible cultural position presenting itself as no position at all.

1. **Apply the positional framework** - For any piece of writing, surface three questions:
   - *What is my relationship to this subject?* - Am I inside it (participant, practitioner), adjacent to it (observer with access), or outside it (analyst, theorist)? Each position enables different claims.
   - *What can I legitimately claim from this position?* - Where does my authority come from: lived experience, professional expertise, systematic study, cultural membership? Name the source.
   - *What am I unable to see from where I stand?* - Every position has blind spots. Name them. This is not a disclaimer - it is structural honesty that shapes what the writing can and should attempt.

2. **Make invisible positions visible** - Generic unmarked voice defaults to dominant-culture register. Prose that asserts a claim about universally desirable qualities tends to speak from an unmarked tradition. Prose that names the position from which it speaks turns the same claim into situated evidence anchored to where the writer stands.

3. **Check for simulated position** - AI writing's characteristic failure is generating plausible positional language without actual stakes. Watch for:
    - Position claimed but never demonstrated through specific detail
    - Authority asserted generically ("as a professional in this field") without situated evidence
    - Blind spots acknowledged as a rhetorical gesture rather than as a genuine constraint on what follows

    If the writer has not specified their relationship to the subject, ask. Do not construct a generic authorial voice.

## Questions

- Does the writing sound like one person across the whole piece, or do passages drift into different sensibilities — casual to formal, certain to hesitant, direct to indirect — without clear reason?
- What sentence-level patterns reveal the voice's character: directness or indirection, certainty or qualification, formality or casualness, warmth or distance?
- Where does the voice slip into corporate-neutral default — language any institution could publish — when the situation called for the writer's specific register?
- What is the writer's relationship to this subject — inside it as participant, adjacent as observer with access, or outside as analyst — and what can the writer legitimately claim from that position?
- What can the writer not see from where they stand? What would someone from a different position notice that the writer cannot?
- Where does the prose claim authority generically, or speak from an unmarked viewpoint that defaults to dominant-culture register without naming itself as such?

## Quality Criteria

When voice and stance are sound:

- [ ] The writing sounds like one person across the whole piece. The sensibility holds even as the surface modulates for context.
- [ ] The voice carries specific character — directness, qualification, formality, warmth — chosen to fit the situation.
- [ ] No passage falls into corporate-neutral default where the writer's specific register would do the work.
- [ ] The writer's relationship to the subject is named, and what the writer can legitimately claim from that position is anchored in specific, situated evidence.
- [ ] The blind spots of the writer's position are named openly, as constraints on what the prose can attempt.
- [ ] Where the prose speaks from a culturally specific viewpoint, the position is acknowledged as one position among others.
