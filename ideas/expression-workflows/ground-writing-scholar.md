# What Writing Is, Across Traditions

## Common Ground (Cross-Tradition)

Writers across traditions converge on several deep principles about what writing is and what it demands, despite vast differences in style, language, and literary culture.

### 1. Writing is an act of making the invisible visible

Writers universally describe writing as surfacing what exists but cannot otherwise be seen or said.

- "Writing novels is about facing death." -- Yoko Ogawa (Japan), via *BookNerd Tokyo*, discussing her conversations with Jungian psychologist Hayao Kawai
- "I write to unfold, to unravel things." -- Annie Ernaux (France), *Scroll.in* interview on her Nobel Prize
- "Fiction is humanity's last collective frontier in honest storytelling." -- Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie (Nigeria), UNESCO Courier interview, January 2026

### 2. Writing requires the writer's full being -- not just technique

The act is not separable from the person. Writers describe it as demanding total presence, not professional skill alone.

- "I don't have any plan. I'm just writing, and while I'm writing, strange things happen very naturally, very automatically." -- Haruki Murakami (Japan), *Business Times* interview, February 2026
- "When suddenly 'I' am not really there, thinking about me, but all of my energy is being used to try to figure out how to make the story better -- I'm fully in it." -- George Saunders (USA), *Orion Magazine*, November 2022
- "I always feel I need to be very truthful when I write." -- Hanan al-Shaykh (Lebanon), *Literary Hub*, June 2019

### 3. Writing serves as witness -- it records what would otherwise be lost

Multiple traditions frame the writer as someone who refuses to let experience disappear unrecorded.

- "A man met his match here." -- Chinua Achebe (Nigeria), via *The Marginalian*, retelling a tortoise parable about leaving marks in the sand so others know a struggle happened
- Elena Poniatowska (Mexico) built her entire body of work on giving voice to "the humblest elements of Mexican society" -- those whom official narratives erase. Source: *Rain Taxi* interview
- Ngugi wa Thiong'o (Kenya) wrote his fifth novel on squares of toilet paper in a maximum-security prison, insisting that the story must exist even when the state tries to silence it. Source: *Milkweed Editions / Between the Covers*, April 2023

### 4. Writing is a form of action, not waiting

Writers across traditions describe writing as intervention in the world -- not passive reflection on it.

- "For me, the word 'writing' is the exact opposite of the word 'waiting.' Instead of waiting, there is writing." -- Roberto Bolano (Chile), email interview with Carmen Boullosa, 2001, collected in *The Last Interview*
- "Where journalism tells us what happened, fiction tells us how it felt." -- Chimamanda Ngozi Adichie (Nigeria), UNESCO Courier, January 2026
- Poniatowska continued writing novels, nonfiction, and journalism into her 90s, saying it "gave her strength and made her feel lucky." Source: Library of Congress PALABRA Archive interview, March 2023

### 5. Writing demands truthfulness over correctness

Not factual accuracy, but fidelity to experience -- writers insist on this distinction consistently.

- "Writing is a means to seek not truth, but what's real." -- Annie Ernaux (France), *Scroll.in* Nobel Prize profile
- Hanan al-Shaykh doesn't deliberately write to challenge taboos -- rather, she "lays bare everything that society tries to hide." Source: *Literary Hub*, June 2019
- "I hope that they find it literature. Just as you read Dickens to know London or Balzac to know Paris, there is a real joy." -- Naguib Mahfouz (Egypt), unpublished *Maclean's* interview via Jonathan Curiel's blog

### 6. The struggle itself is the meaning

Writers describe the difficulty of writing not as obstacle but as the point -- the resistance is generative.

- Achebe's tortoise parable: the value is not in winning the fight but in making sure the evidence of struggle remains. Source: *The Marginalian*
- "The truth is, I don't believe all that much in writing. Starting with my own." -- Roberto Bolano (Chile), yet he wrote compulsively until his death. Source: *Biblioklept* / *Bomb* magazine
- Murakami describes novel-writing as requiring him to "establish a fixed routine... creating this solid base is absolutely crucial. If I screw that up, my strength may give out partway through." Source: *Penguin UK*, November 2022

### 7. Reading and writing are inseparable

Writers from every tradition describe reading as the other half of writing -- not preparation for it, but the same activity.

- "Reading is like thinking, like praying, like talking to a friend, like expressing your ideas, like listening to other people's ideas, like listening to music (oh yes), like looking at the view, like taking a walk on the beach." -- Roberto Bolano (Chile), collected in *Big Other*
- "Reading is pleasure and happiness to be alive or sadness to be alive and above all it's knowledge and questions." -- Roberto Bolano (Chile), same source
- Yoko Ogawa's encounter with Anne Frank's diary at the same age Anne wrote it "touched the young Ogawa like an electric bolt" and shaped her entire writing life. Source: *The Telegraph*, September 2024

## Tradition-Specific Insights

### Japanese tradition

**1. Writing as descent, not assertion.**
Japanese writers describe writing as going *down* into something rather than *out* toward an audience. Murakami's process is famously subterranean -- he describes entering a story without a plan and letting "strange things happen very naturally, very automatically." This is not spontaneity as Western writing advice frames it (brainstorming, freewriting). It is closer to meditative surrender. Source: *Business Times*, February 2026

**2. Writing as facing death.**
Both Yoko Ogawa and novelist Hiromi Kawakami have independently said "writing novels is about facing death." Ogawa explored this through her conversations with Jungian psychologist Hayao Kawai, treating the novel as a space where mortality can be held and examined without resolution. Anglo-American craft advice rarely frames writing this way -- it emphasizes communication, persuasion, clarity. The Japanese framing suggests writing's purpose is ontological, not rhetorical. Source: *BookNerd Tokyo*

**3. Simplicity as window, not reduction.**
Murakami famously wrote his first novel in English first, then "translated" it back to Japanese, stripping away the ornamental conventions of Japanese literary prose. His goal: "I want to open a window in their souls." Simplicity here is not about readability metrics -- it is about creating transparency between writer and reader. Source: *The Guardian*, November 2022

### Latin American tradition

**1. Literature as a vast ecosystem, not a hierarchy of masterpieces.**
Bolano insisted that "Literature is a vast forest and the masterpieces are the lakes, the towering trees or strange trees, the lovely eloquent flowers, the hidden caves, but a forest is also made up of ordinary trees, patches of grass, puddles, clinging vines, mushrooms and little wildflowers." This ecological metaphor resists the Anglo-American habit of ranking writing by "quality" and instead values the ecosystem. Source: *Big Other*

**2. Writing as testimony for the voiceless.**
Poniatowska's work bridges fiction, journalism, and oral history. Her *Massacre in Mexico* gave voice to survivors of the 1968 Tlatelolco massacre. Valeria Luiselli's *Lost Children Archive* studied the refugee crisis "obliquely, reading about other historical moments of children's mass displacement, amassing a reader's archive of loss." Latin American writers treat writing not just as personal expression but as collective memory-making. Sources: *Rain Taxi*; *Longreads*, February 2019

**3. Ironic self-awareness about writing itself.**
Bolano writes: "The truth is, I don't believe all that much in writing. Starting with my own." He compares writing to bank robbery and being a gigolo -- "Unfortunately, the child grows up, the bank robber is killed, the director runs out of money, the gigolo gets sick and then there's no other choice but to write." This irreverence toward the act of writing is distinctly Latin American -- it punctures the sanctimony that Anglo-American craft advice often carries. Source: *Biblioklept* / *Bomb*

### African tradition

**1. The writer as restorer of stolen narrative.**
Achebe's foundational argument: colonialism did not just take land and labor but stole the story. The writer's job is restoration. "African Literature as Restoration of Celebration" -- the title of his 1990 essay -- frames writing not as individual expression but as civilizational repair. Source: Semantic Scholar / *Kunapipi*

**2. Language is not neutral infrastructure.**
Ngugi wa Thiong'o made the political decision to stop writing in English in 1977, arguing that "Language as culture is... mediating between me and my own self; between my own self and other selves; between me and nature." Writing in a colonizer's language means writing through a colonizer's consciousness. This insight -- that the language you write in shapes what you can think -- is largely absent from Anglo-American writing advice, which treats English as transparent medium. Source: *Decolonising the Mind* (1986), quoted in JHU Africana Studies, May 2025

**3. Fiction as the last space for honest storytelling.**
Adichie argues that fiction now has an "increasingly urgent role" because it can tell truths that journalism, hemmed in by objectivity norms and political pressure, cannot. Fiction possesses "a unique power to take us into the lives and motivations of other people." This is not empathy as writing-workshop platitude; it is a claim about fiction as a political epistemology. Source: UNESCO Courier, January 2026

### French tradition

**1. Writing as ethnology of the self.**
Annie Ernaux describes her approach as "an ethnology of myself" -- studying her own life with the detachment and rigor of an anthropologist. This is distinct from Anglo-American memoir, which tends toward confession or self-expression. Ernaux seeks to make personal experience into social data. "The value of a text is indeed the newness it brings to your vision of viewing things." Source: *Scroll.in*

**2. Seeking what's real, not what's true.**
Ernaux's distinction between truth and reality is philosophically precise: "Writing is a means to seek not truth, but what's real." Truth implies propositional correctness; reality implies texture, sensation, the thing itself. Her Nobel lecture describes writing as finding "the sentence that will give me the freedom and the firmness to speak without trembling." Source: Nobel Lecture, December 2022; *Scroll.in*

**3. Self-effacement as literary method.**
Marguerite Yourcenar's prefaces exemplify what scholar Colette Gaudin calls "genesis as self-effacement" -- the writer disappears into the work. Yourcenar's *Memoirs of Hadrian* required decades of research and revision not to display erudition but to become transparent to the historical consciousness she inhabited. "The true birthplace is that wherein for the first time one looks intelligently upon oneself; my first homelands have been books." Source: *Memoirs of Hadrian*; Gaudin (1985), *Studies in 20th Century Literature*

### Arabic tradition

**1. Writing as laying bare what society hides.**
Hanan al-Shaykh began writing as a child "as a way of being sure of her own existence." She deliberately avoids classical Arabic in dialogue, using colloquial speech because that is how people actually talk. Her work "lays bare everything that society tries to hide" -- not to scandalize but because concealment is a form of violence. Source: *Literary Hub*, June 2019

**2. Literature must first be literature.**
Mahfouz insisted that political and social dimensions of writing are secondary to its artistic quality: "I hope that they find it literature." He took "freedom in treatment" of historical and religious themes, asserting that creative latitude and social responsibility coexist -- neither dominates. Source: Jonathan Curiel's blog (unpublished *Maclean's* interview); *Paris Review* interview summary

**3. Storytelling as civilizational continuity.**
The Arabic literary tradition connects contemporary fiction to *One Thousand and One Nights* -- storytelling as survival strategy. Al-Shaykh's retelling of Scheherazade's tales frames the original heroine as "the first feminist" -- someone who literally writes herself out of a death sentence. The tradition frames narrative as existentially necessary, not decorative. Source: NPR / *Weekend Edition Sunday*, June 2013

## What AI Writing Lacks

Based on what writers across traditions say writing IS, here is what AI writing systematically fails to achieve -- with specifics.

### 1. Writing-as-thinking (the cognitive byproduct)

Writers consistently describe a "more articulate version of myself" that emerges through the act of writing (Saunders). Ernaux calls it "unfolding." Murakami enters a story without a plan and discovers what it means. AI produces text without undergoing transformation. The output exists, but no consciousness was changed in its production. One academic writer interviewed by Flynn Devine put it directly: "writing is the main part of thinking... you need to write to think deeply... it helps you get more clarity." Source: Saunders via LinkedIn, February 2026; Devine, *Substack*, May 2025

### 2. Strategic omission and silence

As Laura Hartenberger argues, "AI is trained to generate phrase patterns; it's not trained to generate silence." Human writing achieves meaning through what it *doesn't* say -- the pause, the gap, the thing unsaid. Dialogue works when characters *avoid* direct responses. AI fills every gap. It cannot choose to withhold. Source: *CRAFT Literary*, March 2025

### 3. Embodied specificity

AI produces "gentle breeze" where a human writer would produce the particular sensation of wind on a specific body in a specific place. Hartenberger notes AI "will rarely produce truly unexpected, ultranuanced, pleasantly divergent, or potently obtuse word pairings." The issue is not vocabulary but the absence of a body that has felt things. Source: *CRAFT Literary*, March 2025

### 4. Risk and vulnerability

A ghostwriter observed of AI output: "There was nothing wrong with it, except for the black hole where their soul should have been." The client was "raw and outspoken and opinionated" but AI "was hiding all of that." AI writing is constitutionally safe -- it cannot take the risks that make writing matter because it has nothing at stake. Source: Nigel, *Medium*, January 2026

### 5. The writer's mortality

Japanese writers frame writing as "facing death." Achebe's tortoise leaves marks because it is dying. Bolano wrote against his own death from liver failure. The urgency that mortality gives to human writing -- the knowledge that this sentence might be among your last -- is absent from AI, which operates in infinite time with no stakes. Writing that matters carries the pressure of finitude.

### 6. Position and accountability

Ngugi wrote in Gikuyu at the cost of imprisonment. Poniatowska documented massacres the Mexican state denied. Al-Shaykh wrote about women's bodies in a culture that forbids it. AI has no position -- it writes from everywhere and therefore from nowhere. As Valeria Luiselli argues, "Denouncing the things that we witness is a task that requires constant self-awareness, knowing where we are writing from." AI cannot know where it writes from. Source: Luiselli, CCCB interview, November 2019

### 7. Linguistic consciousness

Ngugi's argument that "the language you write in shapes what you can think" exposes a fundamental AI limitation: AI treats language as a statistical pattern, not as culture, identity, or consciousness. It cannot choose to write in Gikuyu as a political act. It cannot feel the weight of writing in a colonizer's tongue. It flattens linguistic choice into style preference.

## Technique Implications

From this synthesis, here are five techniques or framing shifts that writing skills should incorporate -- areas the current expression plugin underweights.

### 1. Writing-as-discovery (not writing-as-communication)

**What writers are pointing to:** Murakami writes without a plan. Ernaux "unfolds." Saunders describes losing himself in revision. Writing is a way of finding out what you think, not transcribing what you already know.

**What the current plugin does:** The expression plugin's techniques largely assume the writer already knows what they want to say and needs help saying it more clearly (hedges, active voice, parallel structure). This is a communication-first model.

**How a skill could address it:** A technique that helps the writer *discover* their argument through drafting -- encouraging exploratory prose, tolerating ambiguity in early drafts, and recognizing when a draft is teaching the writer something they didn't expect. Frame revision as the site where meaning emerges, not where mistakes are corrected.

### 2. Strategic silence and omission

**What writers are pointing to:** Al-Shaykh uses colloquial speech to expose gaps between what people say and what they mean. Ernaux distinguishes truth from reality. Japanese writers describe descent rather than assertion. Great writing achieves power through what it *withholds*.

**What the current plugin does:** Most techniques optimize for adding -- more clarity, more precision, more structure. No technique addresses the craft of *removing* or the power of leaving something unsaid.

**How a skill could address it:** A technique focused on subtractive craft -- identifying what can be removed to create tension, trust, or space for the reader's own cognition. Teaching the difference between vagueness (failure of thought) and productive ambiguity (invitation to thought).

### 3. Positional awareness (where you write from)

**What writers are pointing to:** Ngugi's language politics. Poniatowska's commitment to testimony. Luiselli's "knowing where we are writing from." Adichie's "danger of a single story." Writing is always situated -- the writer's position shapes what can be said and how.

**What the current plugin does:** Treats prose as position-independent. A sentence is clear or unclear regardless of who writes it or for whom. This ignores that the same sentence means different things depending on who says it.

**How a skill could address it:** A technique that asks the writer to articulate their position relative to their subject -- their authority, their limitations, their stakes. Not as disclaimer but as structural decision that shapes what the writing can and should do.

### 4. Embodied specificity over abstract precision

**What writers are pointing to:** Saunders on "particularization" -- moving from concept to specific description. Ernaux on "the reality of the body." Al-Shaykh on colloquial speech. Mahfouz on knowing London through Dickens. Writers insist that specificity comes from contact with the world, not from vocabulary.

**What the current plugin does:** Techniques address word-level precision (active voice, concrete nouns) but not the deeper question of *what makes a detail specific*. A "concrete noun" can still be generic.

**How a skill could address it:** A technique that trains the writer to ground claims in sensory, situated, particular experience -- not "use concrete nouns" but "write from a body that has been in the room." Push writers to replace category descriptions with witnessed detail.

### 5. Writing as resistance to erasure

**What writers are pointing to:** Achebe's "restoration of celebration." Ngugi's prison-cell novel. Poniatowska's oral histories. The Arabic storytelling tradition where narrative is survival. Writing is not just expression -- it is an act of insisting that something exists, matters, and must not be forgotten.

**What the current plugin does:** Frames writing as communication and persuasion. The stakes are effectiveness -- did the reader understand? Was the argument compelling? This misses writing's deeper function as preservation, witness, and defiance.

**How a skill could address it:** A technique that asks: what would be lost if this were not written? What does this writing preserve, witness, or insist upon? This reframes the writer's motivation from "how do I say this well?" to "why must this be said at all?" -- which transforms the prose from the inside.
